Case Applications/ general management

The cases that keep coming back

Dr. Yan’s questions ask you to analyse a scenario with a named framework. Here are the recurring cases, the framework that cracks each, and how to structure a full-credit answer.

Group Decision Making
The Challenger Disaster
Engineers raised concerns about O-ring performance in cold temperatures, but under schedule pressure the launch group reached consensus to proceed. The shuttle broke apart 73 seconds after launch.

Framework that applies

GroupthinkConformity (Asch)

How to crack it

  • Name groupthink: a cohesive group’s drive for unanimity overrode realistic appraisal of the risk.
  • Identify symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, collective rationalisation of the risk, self-censorship and direct pressure on the dissenting engineers, mindguards filtering bad news, illusion of unanimity.
  • Link to the outcome: consensus was valued over the data, so a catastrophic launch decision went unchallenged.
Full-credit move: don’t just say "they didn’t listen" — map at least three named symptoms onto specific facts of the case.
Change Management
Blockbuster vs. Netflix
Blockbuster dominated video rental on a store-and-late-fee model, declined to adapt, and went bankrupt. Netflix shifted from mail DVDs to streaming and reshaped the industry.

Framework that applies

Planned vs Unplanned ChangeKotter’s 8 StepsResistance to Change

How to crack it

  • Contrast proactive (planned) vs reactive (unplanned) change: Netflix led change; Blockbuster reacted too late.
  • Blockbuster’s failure: no sense of urgency, complacency, a strong culture as a barrier to change (institutionalisation around late fees).
  • Netflix through Kotter: urgency about digital, a vision (streaming), empowering action, anchoring the new model in culture.
Full-credit move: use at least two or three Kotter steps by name and tie each to a concrete decision.
Groups · Power of Roles
Zimbardo’s Stanford Prison Experiment
Students randomly assigned to "guard" or "prisoner" roles rapidly assimilated to them — guards grew abusive, prisoners passive — forcing the study to end early.

Framework that applies

Roles & AssimilationNormsSocial Identity

How to crack it

  • Show how assigned roles and role expectations overrode individual personality — behaviour followed the position, not the person.
  • Connect to emergent norms and social identity: people enacted the in-group ("guards") identity.
Full-credit move: use it to argue the "situation > disposition" point — structure and roles shape behaviour.
Decision Biases · Ethics
The Manager Who Can’t See Their Own Lapse
A manager rationalises a questionable expense claim as "normal", genuinely not perceiving it as unethical, and is confident they’d never cross a line.

Framework that applies

Dunning–KrugerOverconfidenceSelf-serving bias

How to crack it

  • Apply "unskilled and unaware": lacking the metacognition to judge their own ethics, they can’t see the lapse.
  • Add overconfidence and self-serving rationalisation: they over-trust their own judgment and reframe the act to protect self-image.
Full-credit move: explicitly connect a decision-bias concept to the ethics failure — that linkage is the point.
Culture Change
Satya Nadella Reshapes Microsoft (2014)
Nadella shifted Microsoft from a rigid "know-it-all" culture to a "learn-it-all" growth mindset, reviving collaboration and cloud growth.

Framework that applies

Strong Culture & ChangeSchein’s LevelsKotter

How to crack it

  • Strong cultures are long-lasting but changeable with time and effort — Nadella worked the basic assumptions, not just artifacts.
  • Leadership drove new espoused values (growth mindset) and anchored them in everyday practice.
Full-credit move: use Schein’s levels to show change must reach underlying assumptions, not just slogans.
Exam Technique
How to apply any framework

A 4-move template for application questions

  • Name the framework explicitly (the grader is looking for it).
  • Define it in one precise sentence using the class terms.
  • Map each element of the theory onto specific facts of the scenario.
  • Conclude with the "so what" — the managerial implication or outcome.
Grounding beats generality: a SMART-goal answer must use Specific, Measurable, Agreed-upon, Reasonable, Time-bound, not a vague definition.
