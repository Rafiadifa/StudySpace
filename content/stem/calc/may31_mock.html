Problem 1 Continuity & Mixed Partial Derivatives 10 pts
Consider the function below, defined piecewise at the origin.
\[f(x,y)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{xy(x^2-y^2)}{x^2+y^2}, & (x,y)\neq(0,0)\\[6pt] 0, & (x,y)=(0,0).\end{cases}\]
  1. (a)
    Show that \(f\) is continuous at \((0,0)\). 2 pts
    Solution In polar form \(x=r\cos\theta,\,y=r\sin\theta\): \(|f|=r^2\,|\cos\theta\sin\theta(\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta)|\le r^2\to0\) as \(r\to0\).
    \(\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}f(x,y)=0=f(0,0)\) — continuous ✓
  2. (b)
    Using the limit definition, compute \(f_x(0,0)\) and \(f_y(0,0)\). 3 pts
    Solution \(f_x(0,0)=\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{f(h,0)-0}{h}=\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{0}{h}=0\). Similarly \(f_y(0,0)=\lim_{k\to0}\dfrac{f(0,k)}{k}=0\).
    \(f_x(0,0)=0,\qquad f_y(0,0)=0.\)
  3. (c)
    Away from the origin, \(f_x(0,y)=-y\) and \(f_y(x,0)=x\). Use these to compute \(f_{xy}(0,0)\) and \(f_{yx}(0,0)\). 3 pts
    Solution \(f_{xy}(0,0)=\lim_{k\to0}\dfrac{f_x(0,k)-f_x(0,0)}{k}=\lim_{k\to0}\dfrac{-k-0}{k}=-1.\) \(f_{yx}(0,0)=\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{f_y(h,0)-f_y(0,0)}{h}=\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{h-0}{h}=+1.\)
    \(f_{xy}(0,0)=-1\neq f_{yx}(0,0)=+1.\)
  4. (d)
    Your two answers in (c) differ. Why does this not contradict Clairaut's theorem? 2 pts
    Solution Clairaut's theorem requires the mixed partials to be continuous in a neighbourhood of the point. Here they exist everywhere but are not continuous at the origin, so the hypothesis fails and equality is not guaranteed.
    This is the classic counterexample showing continuity of the mixed partials is essential.
Problem 2 Drone Terrain Survey — Critical Points 12 pts
A survey drone maps the elevation of a small valley. The terrain height (in metres) above a reference plane is modelled by
\[h(x,y)=x^3 - 3xy + y^3.\]
  1. (a)
    Compute \(\nabla h\) and find all critical points (where the drone reads zero slope). 3 pts
    Solution \(\nabla h=(3x^2-3y,\;-3x+3y^2)\). Setting both to zero: \(y=x^2\) and \(x=y^2\). Substituting: \(x=x^4\Rightarrow x(x^3-1)=0\Rightarrow x=0\) or \(x=1\).
    Critical points: \((0,0)\) and \((1,1)\).
  2. (b)
    Compute the Hessian \(H\). Classify each critical point as a local max, local min, or saddle. 5 pts
    Solution \(H=\begin{pmatrix}6x&-3\\-3&6y\end{pmatrix}\), \(\det H=36xy-9\).
    At \((0,0)\): \(\det H=-9<0\) → saddle point.
    At \((1,1)\): \(\det H=27>0\) and \(h_{xx}=6>0\) → local minimum.
    \((0,0)\) saddle; \((1,1)\) local min.
  3. (c)
    At the point \((2,1)\), find the direction of steepest ascent and the maximum rate of climb. 2 pts
    Solution \(\nabla h(2,1)=(3\cdot4-3,\;-6+3)=(9,-3)\). Steepest ascent is in the direction of the gradient.
    Direction \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{90}}(9,-3)=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{10}}(3,-1)\); max rate of climb \(=\|\nabla h\|=\sqrt{90}=3\sqrt{10}\approx9.49\).
  4. (d)
    Give the equation of the tangent plane to the terrain at \((1,1)\). 2 pts
    Solution \(h(1,1)=1-3+1=-1\) and \(\nabla h(1,1)=(0,0)\) (it is the local min from part b).
    \(z=-1+0(x-1)+0(y-1)\;\Rightarrow\; z=-1\) (horizontal plane, as expected at a local min).
Problem 3 Multivariable Chain Rule 10 pts
Let \(z=f(x,y)=x^2y\), where \(x\) and \(y\) depend on two parameters:
\[x(s,t)=s\cos t,\qquad y(s,t)=s\sin t.\]
  1. (a)
    Use the chain rule to find \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial s}\) in terms of \(s\) and \(t\). 3 pts
    Solution \(z_x=2xy,\;z_y=x^2\); \(x_s=\cos t,\;y_s=\sin t\). So \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial s}=2xy\cos t+x^2\sin t\). Substituting \(x=s\cos t,y=s\sin t\): \(=2s^2\cos^2t\sin t+s^2\cos^2t\sin t\).
    \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial s}=3s^2\cos^2 t\,\sin t.\)
  2. (b)
    Find \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial t}\) in terms of \(s\) and \(t\). 3 pts
    Solution \(x_t=-s\sin t,\;y_t=s\cos t\). So \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial t}=2xy(-s\sin t)+x^2(s\cos t)=-2s^3\cos t\sin^2 t+s^3\cos^3 t\).
    \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial t}=s^3\cos t\,(\cos^2 t-2\sin^2 t).\)
  3. (c)
    Verify your answer to (a) by substituting first to get \(z(s,t)\) directly, then differentiating. 2 pts
    Solution \(z=x^2y=(s\cos t)^2(s\sin t)=s^3\cos^2 t\sin t\). Then \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial s}=3s^2\cos^2 t\sin t\) — matches part (a) ✓.
  4. (d)
    Evaluate \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial s}\) at \((s,t)=(2,\pi/4)\). 2 pts
    Solution \(\cos^2(\pi/4)=\tfrac12,\;\sin(\pi/4)=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\). So \(3(4)(\tfrac12)(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2})=\dfrac{6}{\sqrt2}=3\sqrt2\).
    \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial s}\Big|_{(2,\pi/4)}=3\sqrt{2}\approx4.243.\)
Problem 4 Pollutant Dispersion & Directional Change 8 pts
A pollutant disperses across a lake surface. Its concentration (in ppm) at position \((x,y)\) is given below; a sensor drifts through the point \(P=(1,1)\).
\[C(x,y)=100\,e^{-(x^2+2y^2)}.\]
  1. (a)
    Compute \(\nabla C\) at \(P=(1,1)\). 3 pts
    Solution \(C_x=-200x\,e^{-(x^2+2y^2)},\quad C_y=-400y\,e^{-(x^2+2y^2)}\). At \((1,1)\), \(e^{-(1+2)}=e^{-3}\).
    \(\nabla C(1,1)=-200e^{-3}\,(1,\,2)\approx(-9.96,\,-19.91)\) ppm/unit.
  2. (b)
    The sensor moves in the direction \(\mathbf v=(3,4)\). Find the rate of change of concentration it measures at \(P\). 3 pts
    Solution Unit vector \(\mathbf u=\tfrac15(3,4)\). \(D_{\mathbf u}C=\nabla C\cdot\mathbf u=-200e^{-3}(1,2)\cdot\tfrac15(3,4)=-200e^{-3}\cdot\tfrac{11}{5}\).
    \(D_{\mathbf u}C(1,1)=-440e^{-3}\approx-21.9\) ppm per unit distance.
  3. (c)
    In which direction should the sensor move from \(P\) to record the fastest decrease in concentration? 2 pts
    Solution Concentration decreases fastest in the direction of \(-\nabla C\), i.e. along \(+(1,2)\) normalised.
    Direction \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt5}(1,2)\); maximum rate of decrease \(=\|\nabla C\|=200\sqrt5\,e^{-3}\approx22.3\) ppm/unit.
    Both partials are negative, so \(-\nabla C\) points along \(+(1,2)\) — away from the concentration peak at the origin.
