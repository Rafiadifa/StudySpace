Theory Reference · Ch. 12–18

Definitions, the key theorems, and the proof intuitions behind them — one section per chapter, framed the way Prof. Yang asks them. Features his harmonic-series grouping proof and the two pointwise-convergence escape examples he called 'more important than the theorems'.

Limits & Continuity in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

Definition / Idea

A limit \(\lim_{\mathbf x\to\mathbf a}f(\mathbf x)=L\) means \(f\) approaches \(L\) along every path. Continuity at \(\mathbf a\) means the limit exists and equals \(f(\mathbf a)\).

Key theorems
  1. Path test for non-existence: if two paths into \(\mathbf a\) give different limit values, the limit does not exist.
  2. Squeeze / polar bound: if \(|f|\le g(r)\) with \(g(r)\to0\) as \(r\to0\), then the limit is \(0\).
  3. Continuity is preserved under sums, products, compositions of continuous functions.
Proof intuition — step through it
Proof intuition — polar squeeze
1
To show \(\lim_{(x,y)\to0}\dfrac{x^2y}{x^2+y^2}=0\), switch to polar \(x=r\cos\theta,y=r\sin\theta\).
2
\(\left|\dfrac{x^2y}{x^2+y^2}\right|=\left|\dfrac{r^3\cos^2\theta\sin\theta}{r^2}\right|=r\,|\cos^2\theta\sin\theta|\le r.\)
3
As \(r\to0\) the bound \(r\to0\) independent of \(\theta\) — so the limit is \(0\) along every path.
4
Contrast \(\dfrac{xy}{x^2+y^2}\): along \(y=mx\) it equals \(\dfrac{m}{1+m^2}\), path-dependent → no limit.
The polar bound must be independent of \(\theta\); if \(\theta\) survives in the bound, the limit likely fails.
Subtleties & pitfalls

A finite number of paths agreeing proves nothing — only a uniform bound (or all-path argument) proves existence. The path test can only prove non-existence.

How it's asked on the exam

Yang opens differentiability problems by probing continuity at a piecewise origin. Show the polar bound explicitly; a hand-wave loses the rigor points.

Differentiability vs Partials

Definition / Idea

\(f\) is differentiable at \(\mathbf a\) if it admits a good linear approximation: \(f(\mathbf a+\mathbf h)=f(\mathbf a)+\nabla f(\mathbf a)\cdot\mathbf h+o(\lVert\mathbf h\rVert)\). This is STRONGER than the partials merely existing.

Key theorems
  1. Differentiable \(\Rightarrow\) continuous, and all directional derivatives exist and equal \(\nabla f\cdot\mathbf u\).
  2. Sufficient condition: if the partials exist and are continuous near \(\mathbf a\), then \(f\) is differentiable there.
  3. The converse fails: partials can exist where \(f\) is not even continuous.
Proof intuition — step through it
Proof intuition — partials exist but not differentiable
1
\(f(x,y)=\dfrac{xy}{x^2+y^2}\) (0 at origin). Along axes \(f=0\), so \(f_x(0,0)=f_y(0,0)=0\): partials exist.
2
But along \(y=x\), \(f=\tfrac12\neq0=f(0,0)\): \(f\) is not continuous at the origin.
3
Not continuous \(\Rightarrow\) not differentiable, even though both partials exist.
4
Moral: existence of partials is too weak; continuity of partials is the safe sufficient test.
This is the canonical separating example. Differentiability sits strictly between 'partials exist' and 'partials continuous'.
Subtleties & pitfalls

Never infer differentiability from 'the partials exist'. The only easy sufficient test is continuity of the partials.

How it's asked on the exam

The rubber-stress problem asks 'is \(f\) / \(f_x\) / \(f_{xy}\) continuous?' — a differentiability ladder. Answer each rung separately.

Gradient & Its Geometry

Definition / Idea

\(\nabla f\) points in the direction of steepest increase, with magnitude equal to that maximum rate. It is orthogonal to level sets of \(f\).

Key theorems
  1. \(D_{\mathbf u}f=\nabla f\cdot\mathbf u\le\lVert\nabla f\rVert\), with equality when \(\mathbf u\parallel\nabla f\).
  2. \(\nabla f\perp\) the level curve/surface through the point.
  3. At an interior extremum, \(\nabla f=\mathbf 0\) (Fermat's condition).
Proof intuition — step through it
Proof intuition — why gradient ⟂ level set
1
Let \(\gamma(t)\) lie in a level set: \(f(\gamma(t))=c\) constant.
2
Differentiate with the chain rule: \(\nabla f(\gamma)\cdot\gamma'(t)=0\).
3
\(\gamma'\) is tangent to the level set, so \(\nabla f\) is orthogonal to every tangent — hence to the level set.
4
Therefore the steepest-ascent direction is perpendicular to the contours.
This single chain-rule line is the whole proof; Yang expects you to reproduce it.
Subtleties & pitfalls

The 'steepest' interpretation needs differentiability. At a kink the gradient may not exist and the geometry breaks.

How it's asked on the exam

Conceptual sub-parts ask to draw \(\nabla f\) on a contour plot or compare gradient magnitude at two points (denser contours = larger gradient).

Newton's Method — Why It Converges

Definition / Idea

Newton replaces \(F\) by its linearisation at the current guess and solves that exactly; the root of the linearisation is the next guess.

Key theorems
  1. Update: \(\mathbf x_{n+1}=\mathbf x_n-[DF(\mathbf x_n)]^{-1}F(\mathbf x_n)\).
  2. Near a simple root (with \([DF]\) invertible), convergence is quadratic: the error roughly squares each step.
  3. Requires \([DF]\) nonsingular along the iteration; otherwise the step is undefined.
Proof intuition — step through it
Proof intuition — quadratic convergence
1
Taylor-expand \(F\) about the root \(\mathbf r\): \(F(\mathbf x_n)\approx [DF(\mathbf r)](\mathbf x_n-\mathbf r)+O(\lVert\mathbf x_n-\mathbf r\rVert^2)\).
2
Substituting into the update shows the linear term cancels by construction.
3
What remains is the quadratic remainder, so \(\lVert\mathbf x_{n+1}-\mathbf r\rVert\le C\lVert\mathbf x_n-\mathbf r\rVert^2\).
4
Squaring the error each step is why a good guess converges in 3–4 iterations.
The cancellation of the first-order term is the mechanism — that is what 'using the derivative' buys you.
Subtleties & pitfalls

Only local: a far or unlucky start can diverge, cycle, or be thrown off by a singular Jacobian. No global guarantee.

How it's asked on the exam

After a numeric step, Yang asks 'comment on convergence' — cite quadratic convergence and verify \(\lVert F\rVert\) shrank.

Inverse & Implicit Function Theorems

Definition / Idea

Both theorems trade a nonlinear local question for a linear-algebra condition on the Jacobian. If the relevant derivative is invertible/nonzero, the nonlinear object behaves like its linearisation locally.

Key theorems
  1. Inverse FT: \(\det[DF(\mathbf a)]\neq0\Rightarrow F\) has a smooth local inverse near \(\mathbf a\).
  2. Implicit FT: for \(g(x,y)=c\), if \(g_y(\mathbf a)\neq0\) then \(y=y(x)\) locally with \(y'=-g_x/g_y\).
  3. Where the condition fails, the solution set can pinch, cross, or turn vertical.
Proof intuition — step through it
Proof intuition — implicit derivative
1
Assume \(g(x,y(x))=c\) holds identically near the point.
2
Differentiate in \(x\) by the chain rule: \(g_x+g_y\,y'(x)=0\).
3
Solve: \(y'(x)=-g_x/g_y\), valid exactly when \(g_y\neq0\).
4
If \(g_y=0\), the formula blows up — signalling a vertical tangent or a non-smooth point.
The condition \(g_y\neq0\) is literally the denominator — geometry and algebra coincide.
Subtleties & pitfalls

Existential and local: guarantees a function exists but not a formula, and only in a neighbourhood. Says nothing globally.

How it's asked on the exam

The 2025 'single non-smooth point' surface problem: set \(\nabla g=0\) (all partials vanish) and solve for the constant \(\alpha\) making the level set pinch.

Clairaut's Theorem

Definition / Idea

For nice functions the order of partial differentiation doesn't matter: \(f_{xy}=f_{yx}\). 'Nice' = the mixed partials are continuous.

Key theorems
  1. If \(f_{xy}\) and \(f_{yx}\) are continuous on an open set, they are equal there.
  2. Continuity is essential — drop it and equality can fail.
  3. Consequence: the Hessian is symmetric for \(C^2\) functions.
Proof intuition — step through it
Proof intuition — the symmetric difference quotient
1
Consider the second difference \(\Delta=f(a{+}h,b{+}k)-f(a{+}h,b)-f(a,b{+}k)+f(a,b)\).
2
Apply the Mean Value Theorem in \(x\) then \(y\): \(\Delta=f_{xy}(\xi,\eta)\,hk\) for some interior point.
3
Apply MVT in the other order: \(\Delta=f_{yx}(\xi',\eta')\,hk\).
4
As \(h,k\to0\), continuity forces both interior values to the same limit \(\Rightarrow f_{xy}=f_{yx}\).
The proof hinges on continuity at the last step — exactly the hypothesis the counterexample violates.
Subtleties & pitfalls

Without continuity of the mixed partials, equality can fail (the \(xy(x^2-y^2)/(x^2+y^2)\) origin gives \(\pm1\)).

How it's asked on the exam

'Do we have \(f_{xy}(0,0)=f_{yx}(0,0)\)?' — answer with the theorem's hypothesis and, if piecewise, the limit-definition computation.

The Generalized FTC (one family)

Definition / Idea

Green's, Gauss's (divergence), and Stokes' theorems are all the same statement: the integral of a derivative over a region equals an integral of the original over the boundary. \(\int_\Omega d\omega=\int_{\partial\Omega}\omega\).

Key theorems
  1. Gradient theorem: \(\int_\gamma\nabla\phi\cdot d\gamma=\phi(\text{end})-\phi(\text{start})\).
  2. Green/Divergence: \(\iint_R(\nabla\cdot F)\,dA=\oint_{\partial R}F\cdot\mathbf n\,ds\) (outward).
  3. Stokes: \(\iint_S(\nabla\times F)\cdot\mathbf n\,dS=\oint_{\partial S}F\cdot d\gamma\).
Proof intuition — step through it
Why they're one theorem
1
Each says: integrate a 'derivative' (\(\nabla\phi\), \(\nabla\cdot F\), \(\nabla\times F\)) over a \(k\)-dimensional region.
2
That equals integrating the un-differentiated object over the \((k{-}1)\)-dimensional boundary.
3
Boundary orientation (sign / right-hand rule) is the bookkeeping that makes the equality hold.
4
This is the content of the differential-forms identity \(\int_\Omega d\omega=\int_{\partial\Omega}\omega\) — which Yang noted is NOT itself on the exam, only its three classical cases.
Knowing they are one idea helps you pick the right tool: match the dimension of what you can compute easily.
Subtleties & pitfalls

Each requires the field be \(C^1\) on the whole region — a singularity inside (e.g. inverse-square at the origin) voids the theorem; isolate it first.

How it's asked on the exam

Yang's vector-calc problem hands you a hard direct integral so you convert via the right theorem. Differential forms themselves are explicitly excluded.

Conservative Fields & Path Independence

Definition / Idea

A field is conservative if it is the gradient of a potential. Then line integrals depend only on endpoints, and loops integrate to zero.

Key theorems
  1. \(F=\nabla\phi\Rightarrow\) path-independent and \(\oint F\cdot d\gamma=0\).
  2. On a simply-connected domain, \(\nabla\times F=\mathbf 0\Rightarrow F\) conservative.
  3. Curl-free is necessary always, sufficient only without holes.
Proof intuition — step through it
Proof intuition — curl-free yet not conservative
1
The vortex \(F=\dfrac{(-y,x)}{x^2+y^2}\) has \(\nabla\times F=0\) wherever it's defined.
2
But \(\oint F\cdot d\gamma=2\pi\neq0\) around the origin.
3
The domain has a hole at the origin — not simply connected — so curl-free fails to give a potential.
4
Fill the hole (any simply-connected subregion) and a potential reappears.
The topology of the domain is the hidden hypothesis — a favourite 'why doesn't this work?' prompt.
Subtleties & pitfalls

Curl-free does not imply conservative on a domain with holes. Always check simple-connectedness before claiming a potential exists.

How it's asked on the exam

The exact-differential line integral on the final: test curl, build \(\phi\), evaluate endpoints. Mention the simply-connected caveat.

Series & the Harmonic Example

Definition / Idea

A series \(\sum a_n\) is defined as the limit of partial sums \(S_N=\sum_{n\le N}a_n\). It converges iff that limit exists.

Key theorems
  1. Necessary condition: if \(\sum a_n\) converges then \(a_n\to0\) (NOT sufficient).
  2. The harmonic series \(\sum 1/n\) diverges although \(1/n\to0\).
  3. \(p\)-series \(\sum n^{-p}\) converges iff \(p>1\).
Proof intuition — step through it
Prof. Yang's harmonic grouping proof
1
Group the harmonic series: \(1+\tfrac12+(\tfrac13+\tfrac14)+(\tfrac15+\cdots+\tfrac18)+\cdots\).
2
Bound each group below by its smallest term \(\times\) its size: \(\tfrac13+\tfrac14>2\cdot\tfrac14=\tfrac12\), next group \(>4\cdot\tfrac18=\tfrac12\).
3
Every group exceeds \(\tfrac12\), and there are infinitely many groups.
4
So \(S_N\) grows past any bound — the series diverges, even though terms \(\to0\).
Yang presented exactly this grouping. The quotable move: replace each group by its smallest member to get a clean \(\tfrac12\) lower bound.
Subtleties & pitfalls

\(a_n\to0\) is necessary, never sufficient — the harmonic series is the standing counterexample. Don't conclude convergence from vanishing terms.

How it's asked on the exam

'Show \(\sum1/n\) diverges' expects the grouping proof, not an integral-test wave. Reproduce the \(\tfrac12\)-per-group bound.

Pointwise Convergence & Escape Examples

Definition / Idea

\(F_n\to F\) pointwise if \(F_n(x)\to F(x)\) for each fixed \(x\). Yang calls this 'nearsighted' — it sees one point at a time and misses global mass.

Key theorems
  1. Pointwise convergence does NOT permit swapping \(\lim\) with \(\int\) or with \(\tfrac{d}{dx}\).
  2. Two escape examples make this concrete and are 'more important than the theorems' (Yang).
  3. The cure is uniform / dominated convergence, which forbids the escape.
Proof intuition — step through it
Horizontal & vertical escape
1
Horizontal: a unit-area bump centred at \(x=n\). Each fixed \(x\): \(F_n(x)\to0\); but \(\int F_n=1\) always.
2
So \(\int\lim F_n=0\neq1=\lim\int F_n\) — the mass escaped to infinity.
3
Vertical: a triangle of area 1, height \(2^n\), width \(2^{-n}\). Each fixed \(x\): \(F_n(x)\to0\); area stays 1.
4
Same contradiction \(0\neq1\) — mass escaped upward. Both show why pointwise is too weak.
Memorise both pictures and the '0 vs 1' punchline — that pair of numbers is the full-credit answer.
Subtleties & pitfalls

Pointwise limits can lose continuity, integrability swaps, and derivative swaps. It is genuinely insufficient for analysis — that's the lesson, not a technicality.

How it's asked on the exam

Conceptual series sub-part: 'give an example where limit and integral don't commute'. One escape example with the two numbers is complete.
