Newton's Method in ℝⁿ

4 Problems Study: 18:10 – 19:00
50 minutes
Core Formula — Newton's Method (multivariable)
\[ \mathbf{x}_{n+1} = \mathbf{x}_n - [DF(\mathbf{x}_n)]^{-1} F(\mathbf{x}_n) \]

Key Ideas to Remember

  • Compute the Jacobian \([DF]\): the matrix of all partial derivatives of \(F\).
  • F is differentiable at a point if all partials are continuous there (sufficient condition).
  • Linear approximation: \(F(\mathbf{x}_0 + \mathbf{h}) \approx F(\mathbf{x}_0) + [DF(\mathbf{x}_0)]\mathbf{h}\).
  • Each Newton step: invert the Jacobian, then subtract \([DF]^{-1}F\) from current guess.
  • For \(2\times 2\): \(\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{pmatrix}\).
Problem 1 — Warmup: Intersection of Two Curves
Problem 1
Ellipse and Hyperbola (Practice Midterm Style)
Consider the intersection of an ellipse and a hyperbola in the plane. We want to solve \(F(x,y)=\mathbf{0}\) where
\[ F(x,y) = \begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{x^2}{9} + y^2 - 1 \\[6pt] xy - 1 \end{pmatrix}. \] Assume an initial guess of \((x,y) = (3,\, 0.5)\).
  1. (a) Calculate the Jacobian matrix \([DF(3,\, 0.5)]\) and explain why \(F\) is differentiable there. 4 pts
  2. (b) Use linear approximation to estimate \(F(3.01,\, 0.49)\). 2 pts
  3. (c) Perform one iteration of Newton's method starting from \((3,\, 0.5)\) to find a new approximate solution. 4 pts
(a) Jacobian
The partial derivatives of \(F_1 = \tfrac{x^2}{9}+y^2-1\) and \(F_2 = xy-1\) are: \[\frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x}=\frac{2x}{9},\quad \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y}=2y,\quad \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x}=y,\quad \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial y}=x.\]
\[[DF(3,\tfrac{1}{2})] = \begin{pmatrix} \tfrac{2}{3} & 1 \\ \tfrac{1}{2} & 3 \end{pmatrix}\]
All partial derivatives are polynomials, hence continuous everywhere. Therefore \(F\) is differentiable at \((3, 0.5)\).
(b) Linear Approximation
First evaluate: \(F(3,\tfrac{1}{2}) = \begin{pmatrix} 1/4 \\ 1/2 \end{pmatrix}\). Then with \(\mathbf{h} = \begin{pmatrix}0.01\\-0.01\end{pmatrix}\): \[F(3.01,0.49)\approx \begin{pmatrix}1/4\\1/2\end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}2/3&1\\1/2&3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0.01\\-0.01\end{pmatrix}\]
\[F(3.01,0.49)\approx \begin{pmatrix} 1/4 - 1/300 \\ 1/2 - 1/40 \end{pmatrix} \approx \begin{pmatrix} 0.2467 \\ 0.475 \end{pmatrix}\]
(c) One Newton Step
Let \(A = [DF(3,\tfrac12)]\). \(\det A = \tfrac23 \cdot 3 - 1 \cdot \tfrac12 = 2 - \tfrac12 = \tfrac32.\) \[A^{-1} = \frac{2}{3}\begin{pmatrix}3&-1\\-1/2&2/3\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2&-2/3\\-1/3&4/9\end{pmatrix}\] Update step: \(A^{-1}F(3,\tfrac12)=\begin{pmatrix}2&-2/3\\-1/3&4/9\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1/4\\1/2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1/6\\5/36\end{pmatrix}\)
\[\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}3\\1/2\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}1/6\\5/36\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}17/6\\13/36\end{pmatrix}\approx \begin{pmatrix}2.833\\0.361\end{pmatrix}\]
This step moves the guess toward the intersection where \(F(x,y)\approx\mathbf{0}\).
Problem 2 — Unit Circle and Line (Midterm 2025 Style)
Problem 2
Circle–Line Intersection with Polar Coordinates
We want to find the intersection of the unit circle and the line \(x=y\). Define
\[F(x,y)=\begin{pmatrix} x^2+y^2-1 \\ x-y \end{pmatrix}.\] We guess \((x,y)=(1,1)\) as an initial point.
  1. (a) Compute the Jacobian matrix at \((1,1)\) and explain why \(F\) is differentiable there. 4 pts
  2. (b) Estimate \(F(1.001,\, 1.002)\) using linear approximation. 2 pts
  3. (c) Compute the partial derivatives of \(F\) with respect to polar coordinates: \(D_r F\) and \(D_\theta F\). 4 pts
  4. (d) Starting from \((1,1)\), perform one step of Newton's method. Is the new point closer to the true solution? 3 pts
(a) Jacobian at (1,1)
\[[DF(x,y)]=\begin{pmatrix}2x&2y\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\]
\[[DF(1,1)]=\begin{pmatrix}2&2\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\]
All partial derivatives are polynomials — continuous everywhere — so \(F\) is differentiable at \((1,1)\).
(b) Linear Approximation
\(F(1,1)=\begin{pmatrix}1\\ 0\end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{h}=\begin{pmatrix}0.001\\0.002\end{pmatrix}\).
\[F(1.001,1.002)\approx\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}2&2\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0.001\\0.002\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1.006\\-0.001\end{pmatrix}\]
(c) Polar Derivatives
Substitute \(x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta\): \(F=\begin{pmatrix}r^2-1\\r(\cos\theta-\sin\theta)\end{pmatrix}\).
\[D_r F = \begin{pmatrix}2r\\\cos\theta-\sin\theta\end{pmatrix},\qquad D_\theta F = \begin{pmatrix}0\\-r(\sin\theta+\cos\theta)\end{pmatrix}\]
(d) One Newton Step
\(\det[DF(1,1)]=2(-1)-2(1)=-4\). \[[DF(1,1)]^{-1}=\frac{1}{-4}\begin{pmatrix}-1&-2\\-1&2\end{pmatrix}=\frac{1}{4}\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\1&-2\end{pmatrix}\] Update: \(\frac{1}{4}\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\1&-2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1/4\\1/4\end{pmatrix}\).
\[\mathbf{x}_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix}1/4\\1/4\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}3/4\\3/4\end{pmatrix}\]
The true solution is \((\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2},\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2})\approx(0.707,0.707)\). We went from distance \(\sqrt{2}\approx1.414\) to the origin → closer to the true answer ✓
Problem 3 — New Scenario: Two Parabolas
Problem 3
Intersection of a Circle and Parabola
We want to find intersection points of the circle \(x^2+y^2=4\) and the parabola \(y=x^2-2\). Define
\[F(x,y)=\begin{pmatrix}x^2+y^2-4\\y-x^2+2\end{pmatrix}.\] Use initial guess \((x_0,y_0)=(1,\,-1)\).
  1. (a) Compute the Jacobian \([DF(1,-1)]\) and state why \(F\) is differentiable everywhere. 4 pts
  2. (b) Use linear approximation to estimate \(F(1.02,\,-0.98)\). 2 pts
  3. (c) Perform one Newton step to find \(\mathbf{x}_1\). Verify qualitatively that the new point is a better guess. 4 pts
(a) Jacobian
\([DF(x,y)]=\begin{pmatrix}2x&2y\\-2x&1\end{pmatrix}\).
\[[DF(1,-1)]=\begin{pmatrix}2&-2\\-2&1\end{pmatrix}\]
All entries are polynomials, so continuous everywhere → \(F\) is differentiable everywhere.
(b) Linear Approximation
First: \(F(1,-1)=\begin{pmatrix}1+1-4\\-1-1+2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-2\\0\end{pmatrix}\). With \(\mathbf{h}=\begin{pmatrix}0.02\\0.02\end{pmatrix}\): \[\begin{pmatrix}2&-2\\-2&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0.02\\0.02\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\-0.02\end{pmatrix}\]
\[F(1.02,-0.98)\approx\begin{pmatrix}-2\\0\end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}0\\-0.02\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-2\\-0.02\end{pmatrix}\]
(c) Newton Step
\(\det A = 2(1)-(-2)(-2)=2-4=-2\). \[A^{-1}=\frac{1}{-2}\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-1/2&-1\\-1&-1\end{pmatrix}\] Update: \(A^{-1}F(1,-1)=\begin{pmatrix}-1/2&-1\\-1&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}-2\\0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\end{pmatrix}\).
\[\mathbf{x}_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\-3\end{pmatrix}\]
Check: \(F(0,-3)=\begin{pmatrix}0+9-4\\-3-0+2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}5\\-1\end{pmatrix}\). Still not zero, but convergence depends on the basin — the true solution near \((1,-1)\) is \((0, -2)\), and the method is heading there.
Note: Newton's method may take multiple iterations to converge; one step is rarely enough near a poor initial guess.
Problem 4 — Conceptual + Inverse Function Theorem
Problem 4
Differentiability, Invertibility, and Iteration
Consider the function \(F:\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2\) defined by
\[F(x,y)=\begin{pmatrix}e^x\cos y - 1\\e^x\sin y\end{pmatrix}.\]
  1. (a) Compute the Jacobian \([DF(x,y)]\) in general, and evaluate it at \((0,0)\). Is \(F\) differentiable at \((0,0)\)? 3 pts
  2. (b) Is \([DF(0,0)]\) invertible? Compute \([DF(0,0)]^{-1}\). 3 pts
  3. (c) Perform one Newton step from \((x_0, y_0)=(0,0)\). What can you conclude about \((0,0)\) as a solution? 2 pts
  4. (d) The Inverse Function Theorem guarantees that \(F\) is locally invertible near \((0,0)\). What condition on \([DF]\) ensures this? Is that condition met here? 2 pts
(a) Jacobian
\[[DF(x,y)]=\begin{pmatrix}e^x\cos y & -e^x\sin y\\ e^x\sin y & e^x\cos y\end{pmatrix}\]
\[[DF(0,0)]=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}=I\]
All entries are products of smooth (infinitely differentiable) functions, so \(F\) is differentiable everywhere, including at \((0,0)\).
(b) Invertibility
\(\det[DF(0,0)]=\det(I)=1\neq 0\), so yes, \([DF(0,0)]\) is invertible.
\[[DF(0,0)]^{-1}=I^{-1}=I=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}\]
(c) Newton Step from (0,0)
\(F(0,0)=\begin{pmatrix}e^0\cos 0-1\\e^0\sin 0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix}\).
\[\mathbf{x}_1=(0,0)-I^{-1}\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix}=(0,0)\]
Since \(F(0,0)=\mathbf{0}\), the point \((0,0)\) is already an exact solution. Newton's method terminates in one step.
(d) Inverse Function Theorem
The IFT requires \(\det[DF(\mathbf{x}_0)]\neq 0\) — i.e., the Jacobian must be invertible. Here \(\det[DF(0,0)]=1\neq 0\), so the condition is met. Therefore \(F\) is locally invertible near \((0,0)\), and the IFT guarantees a local inverse \(F^{-1}\) near \(F(0,0)=\mathbf{0}\).
