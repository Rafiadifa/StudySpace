Vector Calculus: Which Theorem When
Calc A2 Final23 June 2026
The FTC family (Ch. 15.1–15.5). (1) Gradient theorem: \(\int_C\nabla f\cdot d\mathbf r=f(B)-f(A)\) — path-independent. (2) Green: \(\oint_C\mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf r=\iint_D(\partial_xQ-\partial_yP)\,dA\). (3) Stokes: \(\oint_C\mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf r=\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf F)\cdot d\mathbf S\). (4) Divergence: \(\iint_{\partial V}\mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf S=\iiint_V\nabla\cdot\mathbf F\,dV\). Decision: closed planar curve → Green; closed space curve bounding a surface → Stokes; closed surface → divergence; \(\nabla\times\mathbf F=\mathbf 0\) on a simply-connected domain → conservative, use the gradient theorem.
★ Connected ProblemConservative field → potential → gradient theoremexam-style
Let \(\mathbf F=(2xy+1,\ x^2)\) on \(\mathbb R^2\).
chains: curl test · find a potential · gradient theorem · path-independence
  1. (a)
    Show \(\mathbf F\) is conservative.
    SolutionScalar curl \(\partial_x(x^2)-\partial_y(2xy+1)=2x-2x=0\), and \(\mathbb R^2\) is simply connected, so \(\mathbf F\) is conservative.
    curl \(=0\) ⇒ conservative.
  2. (b)
    Find a potential \(f\) with \(\nabla f=\mathbf F\).
    Solution\(f_x=2xy+1\Rightarrow f=x^2y+x+g(y)\); then \(f_y=x^2+g'(y)=x^2\Rightarrow g'=0\). So \(f=x^2y+x\).
    \(f=x^2y+x\).
  3. (c)
    Evaluate \(\int_C\mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf r\) from \((0,0)\) to \((1,2)\).
    SolutionGradient theorem: \(f(1,2)-f(0,0)=(2+1)-0=3\).
    \(3\).
  4. (d)
    Why doesn't the path matter?
    Solution\(\mathbf F=\nabla f\), so the integral depends only on the endpoints — every path from \((0,0)\) to \((1,2)\) gives \(3\).
    path-independent.
Problem 1Green’s theorem8 pts
Compute \(\oint_C\mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf r\) for \(\mathbf F=(-y,\,x)\), \(C\) the unit circle CCW.
Solution\(\partial_xQ-\partial_yP=1-(-1)=2\), so \(\oint=\iint_D 2\,dA=2(\pi)=2\pi\). (This is the \(2\times\)area trick.)
\(2\pi\).
Problem 2Divergence theorem8 pts
Compute the outward flux \(\iint_S\mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf S\) for \(\mathbf F=(x,y,z)\), \(S\) the unit sphere.
Solution\(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F=3\), so \(\iint_S=\iiint_V 3\,dV=3\cdot\tfrac{4\pi}{3}=4\pi\).
\(4\pi\).
Problem 3Stokes’ theorem8 pts
Compute \(\oint_C\mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf r\) for \(\mathbf F=(-y,\,x,\,z^2)\), \(C\) the unit circle in \(z=0\), CCW from \(+z\).
Solution\(\nabla\times\mathbf F=(0,0,2)\). Cap \(C\) with the flat disk \(S\), \(\mathbf n=(0,0,1)\): \(\oint=\iint_S(0,0,2)\cdot(0,0,1)\,dA=2\pi\).
\(2\pi\).
★ ChallengePick the theorempast-final level
For each scenario, name the theorem that makes it easiest, and why.
chains: read the geometry · choose the theorem · justify
  1. (a)
    A messy \(\mathbf F\) with simple \(\nabla\times\mathbf F\), integrated around a closed space curve.
    SolutionStokes: replace the hard line integral by the flux of \(\nabla\times\mathbf F\) through any capping surface — choose the flat one.
    Stokes.
  2. (b)
    Flux of \(\mathbf F\) out of a closed surface, with simple \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F\).
    SolutionDivergence theorem: convert the surface integral to a volume integral of \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F\).
    Divergence.
  3. (c)
    Work of \(\mathbf F\) around a closed loop in the plane.
    SolutionGreen: \(\oint=\iint_D(\partial_xQ-\partial_yP)\,dA\) over the enclosed region.
    Green.
  4. (d)
    A line integral of \(\nabla f\) along some path.
    SolutionGradient theorem: the answer is just \(f(\text{end})-f(\text{start})\); the path is irrelevant.
    Gradient theorem.
