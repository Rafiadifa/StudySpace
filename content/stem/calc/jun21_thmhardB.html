Pick the theorem, then finish (II). Includes the "flux through one piece", the "closed surface ⇒ curl-flux is 0", and the tangent-field tricks.
★ Connected ProblemFlux through one piece of an ice-cream regionexam-style
Let \(U\) be inside the sphere \(\rho=1\) and above the cone \(\phi=\tfrac{\pi}{3}\) (\(z\ge0\)), and \(\mathbf F=(x,y,z)\).
  1. (a)
    Which theorem relates \(\iiint_U\nabla\cdot\mathbf F\,dV\) to a surface flux, and why?
    SolutionDivergence theorem: \(\iiint_U\nabla\cdot\mathbf F\,dV=\oiint_{\partial U}\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf n\,dS\), because \(\partial U\) (cap + cone) is a closed surface.
    Divergence theorem.
  2. (b)
    Compute \(\iiint_U\nabla\cdot\mathbf F\,dV\).
    Solution\(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F=3\). \(\mathrm{Vol}(U)=\int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^{\pi/3}\!\!\int_0^1\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta=2\pi(1-\cos\tfrac{\pi}{3})\tfrac13=2\pi\cdot\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac13=\tfrac{\pi}{3}\). So \(\iiint 3\,dV=3\cdot\tfrac{\pi}{3}=\pi\).
    \(\pi\).
  3. (c)
    Find the flux of \(\mathbf F\) through the cone part of \(\partial U\).
    Solution\(\mathbf F=(x,y,z)\) is radial, and the cone contains the radial direction, so \(\mathbf F\) is tangent to the cone: flux \(=0\).
    \(0\).
  4. (d)
    Hence find the flux through the spherical cap.
    SolutionTotal flux \(=\pi\) (part b) and cone contributes \(0\), so cap flux \(=\pi\). (Check directly: on \(\rho=1\), \(\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf n=x^2+y^2+z^2=1\), cap area \(=2\pi(1-\cos\tfrac\pi3)=\pi\). ✓)
    \(\pi\).
Problem 1Closed vs open8 pts
(i) For any smooth \(\mathbf G\), what is the flux of \(\nabla\times\mathbf G\) through the whole unit sphere? (ii) For \(\mathbf F=(-y,x,z)\), find the flux of \(\nabla\times\mathbf F\) through the open upper hemisphere (\(z\ge0\)), upward.
Solution(i) \(0\) — the sphere is closed, and the flux of a curl through a closed surface is always \(0\). (ii) The hemisphere is open; its boundary is the unit circle in \(z=0\). Stokes: \(\iint(\nabla\times\mathbf F)\cdot\mathbf n\,dS=\oint_{\text{circle}}\mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf r=\oint -y\,dx+x\,dy=2\pi\). (Or \(\nabla\times\mathbf F=(0,0,2)\), flux \(=2\cdot\) projected area \(=2\pi\).)
(i) \(0\); (ii) \(2\pi\).
Problem 2Choose & execute8 pts
Compute the outward flux of \(\mathbf F=(x,y,2z)\) through the closed solid cylinder \(x^2+y^2\le1,\ 0\le z\le3\).
SolutionClosed surface ⇒ Divergence theorem. \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf F=1+1+2=4\). \(\iiint 4\,dV=4\cdot\mathrm{Vol}=4(\pi\cdot1^2\cdot3)=12\pi.\)
\(12\pi\).
Problem 3Spot the gradient8 pts
Compute \(\int_C\mathbf F\cdot d\mathbf r\) for \(\mathbf F=(yz,\ xz,\ xy)\) from \((1,1,1)\) to \((2,3,4)\).
SolutionRecognize \(\mathbf F=\nabla(xyz)\) (so \(\nabla\times\mathbf F=\mathbf0\)) ⇒ gradient theorem: \(\int_C=xyz\big|_{(1,1,1)}^{(2,3,4)}=2\cdot3\cdot4-1\cdot1\cdot1=24-1=23.\)
\(23\).
