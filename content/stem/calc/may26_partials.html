Partial Derivatives Drill
Calc A2 Final23 June 2026
Quick reference. Partial \(f_x\): differentiate treating \(y\) as constant. Gradient \(\nabla f=(f_x,f_y)\). Directional derivative along a unit vector \(\mathbf u\): \(D_{\mathbf u}f=\nabla f\cdot\mathbf u\). If a direction is given as a non-unit \(\mathbf v\), normalise first: \(\mathbf u=\mathbf v/\lVert\mathbf v\rVert\). Steepest ascent is along \(\nabla f\); its rate is \(\lVert\nabla f\rVert\).

A temperature field is \(T(x,y)=x^2y-y^3\).
chains: partials · gradient · directional derivative · steepest ascent · tangent plane
  1. (a)
    Find \(\nabla T\) and its value at \(P=(2,1)\).
    Solution\(\nabla T=(2xy,\ x^2-3y^2)\); at \(P\): \((4,\ 4-3)=(4,1)\).
    \(\nabla T(2,1)=(4,1)\).
  2. (b)
    Find the directional derivative at \(P\) toward \(\mathbf v=(3,4)\).
    Solution\(\mathbf u=(\tfrac35,\tfrac45)\); \(D_{\mathbf u}T=\nabla T\cdot\mathbf u=4\cdot\tfrac35+1\cdot\tfrac45=\tfrac{16}{5}\).
    \(\tfrac{16}{5}\).
    Normalise \(\mathbf v\) first — the lost-point trap.
  3. (c)
    In which direction does \(T\) rise fastest at \(P\), and how fast?
    SolutionAlong \(\nabla T=(4,1)\); rate \(\lVert\nabla T\rVert=\sqrt{17}\).
    Direction \((4,1)/\sqrt{17}\), rate \(\sqrt{17}\).
  4. (d)
    Write the tangent plane to \(z=T(x,y)\) at \(P\).
    Solution\(T(2,1)=4-1=3\); \(z=3+4(x-2)+1(y-1)\).
    \(z=3+4(x-2)+(y-1)\).
Problem 1 Compute Partials & Gradient 10 pts
Find \(f_x\), \(f_y\), and \(\nabla f\) at the given point.
  1. (a)
    \(f(x,y)=x^3y - 4xy^2\) at \((1,2)\). 3 pts
    Solution \(f_x=3x^2y-4y^2\), \(f_y=x^3-8xy\). At \((1,2)\): \(f_x=6-16=-10\), \(f_y=1-16=-15\).
    \(\nabla f(1,2)=(-10,-15)\).
  2. (b)
    \(f(x,y)=e^{xy}\sin x\) — find \(f_x\). 3 pts
    Solution Product rule in \(x\) (treat \(y\) constant): \(f_x=e^{xy}\cdot y\cdot\sin x+e^{xy}\cos x=e^{xy}(y\sin x+\cos x)\).
    \(f_x=e^{xy}(y\sin x+\cos x)\).
  3. (c)
    \(f(x,y)=\ln(x^2+y^2)\) — find \(\nabla f\). 4 pts
    Solution \(f_x=\dfrac{2x}{x^2+y^2}\), \(f_y=\dfrac{2y}{x^2+y^2}\).
    \(\nabla f=\dfrac{2}{x^2+y^2}(x,y)\).
Problem 2 Directional Derivatives 10 pts
Watch the non-unit-vector trap — normalise before dotting.
  1. (a)
    \(f=x^2y\) at \((1,3)\), direction \(\mathbf v=(3,4)\). 5 pts
    Solution \(\nabla f=(2xy,x^2)=(6,1)\) at \((1,3)\). \(\lVert\mathbf v\rVert=5\Rightarrow\mathbf u=(\tfrac35,\tfrac45)\). \(D_{\mathbf u}f=6\cdot\tfrac35+1\cdot\tfrac45=\tfrac{22}{5}\).
    \(D_{\mathbf u}f=\tfrac{22}{5}=4.4\).
    Forgetting to normalise \(\mathbf v\) is the #1 lost-point here.
  2. (b)
    In which direction does \(f=x^2y\) increase fastest at \((1,3)\), and how fast? 5 pts
    Solution Steepest ascent is along \(\nabla f=(6,1)\); the maximum rate is \(\lVert\nabla f\rVert=\sqrt{37}\).
    Direction \((6,1)/\sqrt{37}\), rate \(\sqrt{37}\).
Problem 3 Mixed Partials & Clairaut 10 pts
Heat field \(T(x,y)=x^2y^3+\sin(xy)\). Verify \(T_{xy}=T_{yx}\).
Solution \(T_x=2xy^3+y\cos(xy)\Rightarrow T_{xy}=6xy^2+\cos(xy)-xy\sin(xy)\).
\(T_y=3x^2y^2+x\cos(xy)\Rightarrow T_{yx}=6xy^2+\cos(xy)-xy\sin(xy)\).
\(T_{xy}=T_{yx}\) ✓ — both continuous, so Clairaut guarantees equality.
Equality is only guaranteed when the mixed partials are continuous; on a piecewise field at a bad point they can differ.
