Computational Reference · Ch. 12–18

Every method on the Calc A2 final, with a worked example you can step through, the limitations to watch for, and how Prof. Yang tends to ask it. Pick a chapter, then a method. Includes his \(u=xy,\,v=y/x\) Jacobian substitution and the harmonic-series grouping proof.

Partial & Directional Derivatives

When to use it

To measure how \(f(x,y)\) changes along an axis (partial) or any unit direction (directional). Foundation for gradient, tangent planes, and optimization.

Procedure
  1. For \(\partial f/\partial x\): differentiate treating \(y\) as a constant. For \(\partial f/\partial y\): hold \(x\) constant.
  2. Assemble the gradient \(\nabla f=(f_x,f_y)\).
  3. For the directional derivative along unit vector \(\mathbf u\): \(D_{\mathbf u}f=\nabla f\cdot\mathbf u\).
  4. If a direction is given as a non-unit vector \(\mathbf v\), normalise first: \(\mathbf u=\mathbf v/\lVert\mathbf v\rVert\).
Worked example — step through it
Example — directional derivative of \(f=x^2y\)
1
\(f(x,y)=x^2y\). Partials: \(f_x=2xy,\; f_y=x^2\).
2
At \((1,3)\): \(\nabla f=(2\cdot1\cdot3,\;1^2)=(6,1)\).
3
Direction \(\mathbf v=(3,4)\), \(\lVert\mathbf v\rVert=5\), so \(\mathbf u=(3/5,4/5)\).
4
\(D_{\mathbf u}f=\nabla f\cdot\mathbf u=6\cdot\tfrac35+1\cdot\tfrac45=\tfrac{18+4}{5}=\tfrac{22}{5}=4.4.\)
The maximum directional derivative at a point is \(\lVert\nabla f\rVert\), attained along \(\nabla f\) itself — here \(\sqrt{37}\).
Limitations & pitfalls

Partials existing does NOT imply differentiability — the partials must also be continuous. A classic trap: a function whose partials exist at the origin but which isn't even continuous there.

How it's asked on the exam

Yang embeds this in a scenario ('temperature/elevation field'). Watch for the non-unit direction trap — forgetting to normalise is the #1 lost-point. State \(\lVert\nabla f\rVert\) when asked for the steepest direction.

Mixed Partials & Clairaut

When to use it

When a problem asks whether \(f_{xy}=f_{yx}\), or to compute second-order partials for a Hessian.

Procedure
  1. Compute \(f_x\), then differentiate it w.r.t. \(y\) to get \(f_{xy}\).
  2. Compute \(f_y\), then differentiate w.r.t. \(x\) to get \(f_{yx}\).
  3. Clairaut: if both mixed partials are continuous near the point, they are equal.
  4. At a suspicious point (e.g. a piecewise origin), compute each mixed partial from the limit definition — they may differ.
Worked example — step through it
Example — Clairaut counterexample
1
\(f(x,y)=\dfrac{xy(x^2-y^2)}{x^2+y^2}\) for \((x,y)\neq(0,0)\), \(f(0,0)=0\).
2
Off-origin one finds \(f_x(0,y)=-y\) and \(f_y(x,0)=x\).
3
\(f_{xy}(0,0)=\lim_{k\to0}\frac{f_x(0,k)-f_x(0,0)}{k}=\lim\frac{-k}{k}=-1.\)
4
\(f_{yx}(0,0)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f_y(h,0)-f_y(0,0)}{h}=\lim\frac{h}{h}=+1.\)
5
\(-1\neq+1\): mixed partials differ because they are not continuous at the origin — Clairaut's hypothesis fails.
This exact function is a recurring exam favourite. The point is conceptual: continuity of the mixed partials is the hypothesis that can break.
Limitations & pitfalls

Equality of mixed partials is only guaranteed under continuity. Blindly assuming \(f_{xy}=f_{yx}\) on a piecewise function loses the whole sub-part.

How it's asked on the exam

Asked as 'do we have \(f_{xy}(0,0)=f_{yx}(0,0)\)?' on a piecewise field (the rubber-stress problem on the 2025 midterm). Always compute from the limit definition at the bad point.

Linear Approximation & Tangent Plane

When to use it

To estimate \(f\) near a known point, or to write the tangent plane to a surface \(z=f(x,y)\).

Procedure
  1. Compute \(f(\mathbf x_0)\) and \(\nabla f(\mathbf x_0)\).
  2. Linear approximation: \(f(\mathbf x_0+\mathbf h)\approx f(\mathbf x_0)+\nabla f(\mathbf x_0)\cdot\mathbf h\).
  3. Tangent plane: \(z=f(\mathbf x_0)+f_x(\mathbf x_0)(x-x_0)+f_y(\mathbf x_0)(y-y_0)\).
  4. For a level surface \(g(x,y,z)=c\), the normal is \(\nabla g\) and the tangent plane is \(\nabla g\cdot(\mathbf x-\mathbf x_0)=0\).
Worked example — step through it
Example — estimate \(f(1.02,1.97)\)
1
\(f(x,y)=x^2y\), base point \((1,2)\). \(f(1,2)=2\).
2
\(\nabla f=(2xy,x^2)\Rightarrow\nabla f(1,2)=(4,1)\).
3
\(\mathbf h=(0.02,-0.03)\): \(\nabla f\cdot\mathbf h=4(0.02)+1(-0.03)=0.08-0.03=0.05.\)
4
\(f(1.02,1.97)\approx 2+0.05=2.05.\) (Exact: \(1.02^2\cdot1.97=2.04938\dots\) — close.)
The approximation error is second-order in \(\lVert\mathbf h\rVert\); good for small steps only.
Limitations & pitfalls

Only accurate for small \(\mathbf h\). Requires differentiability (continuous partials) at the base point — otherwise the 'tangent plane' is meaningless.

How it's asked on the exam

Often part (b) after computing the Jacobian/gradient in (a). State the plane explicitly; don't leave it as a dot product.

Critical Points & the Hessian

When to use it

To classify interior extrema of \(f(x,y)\): local max, local min, or saddle.

Procedure
  1. Solve \(\nabla f=\mathbf 0\) for all critical points.
  2. Form the Hessian \(H=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}f_{xx}&f_{xy}\\f_{yx}&f_{yy}\end{smallmatrix}\right)\).
  3. Compute \(\det H=f_{xx}f_{yy}-f_{xy}^2\) at each critical point.
  4. If \(\det H>0\) and \(f_{xx}>0\): local min; \(\det H>0,\,f_{xx}<0\): local max; \(\det H<0\): saddle; \(\det H=0\): inconclusive.
Worked example — step through it
Example — classify \(h=x^3-3xy+y^3\)
1
\(\nabla h=(3x^2-3y,\,-3x+3y^2)=\mathbf 0\Rightarrow y=x^2,\;x=y^2\Rightarrow x^4=x\Rightarrow x=0,1\).
2
Critical points: \((0,0)\) and \((1,1)\).
3
\(H=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}6x&-3\\-3&6y\end{smallmatrix}\right)\), \(\det H=36xy-9\).
4
At \((0,0)\): \(\det H=-9<0\Rightarrow\) saddle. At \((1,1)\): \(\det H=27>0,\,h_{xx}=6>0\Rightarrow\) local min.
Verified: \(36(1)(1)-9=27>0\). The second-derivative test is decisive here because \(\det H\neq0\).
Limitations & pitfalls

The test fails when \(\det H=0\) — you must then inspect directly. Boundary/constrained extrema need Lagrange or edge analysis, not the Hessian.

How it's asked on the exam

The 'urban heat island' problem: find all \(\nabla=0\) points, classify with the Hessian, then a constrained sub-part on a square boundary. Don't forget endpoints on a constrained edge.

Multivariable Chain Rule

When to use it

When \(z=f(x,y)\) and \(x,y\) themselves depend on other variables \(s,t\). Compute \(\partial z/\partial s\) etc.

Procedure
  1. Draw the dependency tree: \(z\to(x,y)\to(s,t)\).
  2. Sum over every path: \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial s}=\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x}\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial s}+\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial y}\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial s}\).
  3. Substitute the parametrisations to express the answer in \(s,t\).
  4. Optionally verify by substituting first, then differentiating directly.
Worked example — step through it
Example — polar substitution
1
\(z=x^2y\), \(x=s\cos t,\;y=s\sin t\). \(z_x=2xy,\;z_y=x^2\).
2
\(x_s=\cos t,\;y_s=\sin t\). So \(z_s=2xy\cos t+x^2\sin t\).
3
Substitute: \(=2s^2\cos^2t\sin t+s^2\cos^2t\sin t=3s^2\cos^2t\sin t.\)
4
Check by direct substitution: \(z=s^3\cos^2t\sin t\Rightarrow z_s=3s^2\cos^2t\sin t.\;\checkmark\)
Always cross-check with direct substitution when the parametrisation is simple — it catches path-summation slips.
Limitations & pitfalls

Forgetting a path (e.g. when a variable appears in multiple slots) is the classic error. The tree makes every path explicit.

How it's asked on the exam

Standalone or as a step inside an implicit-function problem. Yang likes parametrisations with trig so the final simplification is non-trivial.

Newton's Method in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

When to use it

To solve a nonlinear system \(F(\mathbf x)=\mathbf 0\) iteratively from a starting guess.

Procedure
  1. Write \(F:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^n\) and compute the Jacobian \([DF(\mathbf x)]\).
  2. Evaluate \(F(\mathbf x_n)\) and \([DF(\mathbf x_n)]\) at the current guess.
  3. Solve the linear system \([DF(\mathbf x_n)]\,\Delta=F(\mathbf x_n)\) (or invert the Jacobian).
  4. Update \(\mathbf x_{n+1}=\mathbf x_n-\Delta\). Repeat; check \(\lVert F(\mathbf x_{n+1})\rVert\) shrinks.
Worked example — step through it
Example — one step on a 2×2 system
1
\(F(x,y)=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}x^2-2y\\xy-1\end{smallmatrix}\right)\), guess \((2,1)\). \(F(2,1)=(2,1)\).
2
\([DF]=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}2x&-2\\y&x\end{smallmatrix}\right)\Rightarrow[DF(2,1)]=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}4&-2\\1&2\end{smallmatrix}\right)\), \(\det=10\).
3
\([DF]^{-1}=\tfrac1{10}\left(\begin{smallmatrix}2&2\\-1&4\end{smallmatrix}\right)\). \([DF]^{-1}F(2,1)=\tfrac1{10}(6,2)=(0.6,0.2)\).
4
\(\mathbf x_1=(2,1)-(0.6,0.2)=(1.4,0.8)\). Check \(F(1.4,0.8)=(0.36,0.12)\) — smaller. \(\checkmark\)
The true root satisfies \(x^3=2\Rightarrow x=2^{1/3}\approx1.26\); a second step lands near \((1.265,0.791)\), showing quadratic convergence.
Limitations & pitfalls

Fails if \([DF]\) is singular at a guess (no inverse). Convergence is only local — a poor start can diverge or oscillate.

How it's asked on the exam

Compute the Jacobian (a), one Newton step (b/c), then a convergence/second-step part (d). Keep fractions exact until the final decimal.

Inverse & Implicit Function Theorems

When to use it

To decide if a map is locally invertible (IFT), or whether an equation \(g(x,y)=c\) defines \(y\) as a function of \(x\) locally (implicit FT), and to get the derivative.

Procedure
  1. Inverse FT: \(F\) is locally invertible near \(\mathbf x_0\) iff \(\det[DF(\mathbf x_0)]\neq0\).
  2. Implicit FT: \(g(x,y)=c\) defines \(y(x)\) locally near a point iff \(\partial g/\partial y\neq0\) there.
  3. Implicit derivative: \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{g_x}{g_y}\) (single constraint), generalising to systems via the Jacobian.
  4. Where the relevant partial/determinant vanishes, smoothness can fail (vertical tangent, crossing).
Worked example — step through it
Example — implicit derivative & failure point
1
\(g(x,y)=x^2+y^2-1=0\) (unit circle). \(g_x=2x,\;g_y=2y\).
2
Implicit FT applies wherever \(g_y=2y\neq0\), i.e. away from \((\pm1,0)\).
3
\(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{g_x}{g_y}=-\dfrac{2x}{2y}=-\dfrac{x}{y}.\)
4
At \((\pm1,0)\), \(g_y=0\): the curve has a vertical tangent — \(y\) is NOT a function of \(x\) there.
On the 2025 final, the 'single non-smooth point' problem asks exactly this: where the implicit FT breaks, set the relevant partial/gradient to zero and solve for the constant \(\alpha\).
Limitations & pitfalls

The theorems are local and existential — they guarantee a function exists but rarely give it in closed form. The condition \(\neq0\) is the whole game; check it explicitly.

How it's asked on the exam

Implicit functions are explicitly flagged by Yang as a recurring final topic. Expect 'where does the surface fail to be smooth?' → set the determinant/partial to 0.

Vector Fields: div, curl, classification

When to use it

To analyse a vector field \(F\): find fixed points, classify them, compute divergence/curl, test if it is a gradient field.

Procedure
  1. Fixed points: solve \(F(\mathbf p)=\mathbf 0\).
  2. Linearise: eigenvalues of \([DF(\mathbf p)]\) give the type — all \(\mathrm{Re}>0\) source, all \(<0\) sink, mixed signs saddle, complex spiral/center.
  3. Divergence \(\nabla\cdot F=\operatorname{trace}[DF]\); 2D curl \(=D_1F_2-D_2F_1\); 3D curl is the vector \((D_2F_3-D_3F_2,\,D_3F_1-D_1F_3,\,D_1F_2-D_2F_1)\).
  4. Gradient-field test: \(F=\nabla\phi\Rightarrow\nabla\times F=\mathbf 0\). If curl \(\neq\mathbf 0\), not conservative.
Worked example — step through it
Example — classify a fixed point
1
\(F(x,y)=(x-xy,\,-y+xy)\). Fixed points: \((0,0)\) and \((1,1)\).
2
\([DF]=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}1-y&-x\\y&-1+x\end{smallmatrix}\right)\). At \((0,0)\): \(\left(\begin{smallmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{smallmatrix}\right)\), eigenvalues \(+1,-1\Rightarrow\) saddle.
3
At \((1,1)\): \(\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{smallmatrix}\right)\), char. eq. \(\lambda^2+1=0\Rightarrow\lambda=\pm i\Rightarrow\) center (closed orbits).
4
Divergence \(=\operatorname{trace}=(1-y)+(-1+x)=x-y\): outflow where \(x>y\).
Purely imaginary eigenvalues mean the linearisation predicts rotation; the nonlinear field may spiral, so a center is the linear verdict.
Limitations & pitfalls

Linearisation is only valid near the fixed point and is inconclusive for centers (Re \(=0\)) at the nonlinear level. Curl-free guarantees a potential only on a simply-connected domain.

How it's asked on the exam

Often the 'wind/flow' scenario: divergence → does the cloud disperse; curl → does it spin; gradient test → is there a potential. Verify \(\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=0\) as a check sub-part.

Fubini & Iterated Integrals

When to use it

To evaluate a double/triple integral over a region by iterating one variable at a time.

Procedure
  1. Sketch the region; decide the order \(dx\,dy\) or \(dy\,dx\).
  2. Find the inner limits as functions of the outer variable; outer limits are constants.
  3. Integrate the inner variable first (treat the outer as constant), then the outer.
  4. If the inner integral is intractable, swap the order (Fubini) and redo the limits from the sketch.
Worked example — step through it
Example — order swap to make it doable
1
\(\displaystyle\int_0^1\!\!\int_x^1 e^{y^2}\,dy\,dx\). Inner \(\int e^{y^2}dy\) has no elementary form — swap.
2
Region: \(0\le x\le y\le1\). Reorder: \(\displaystyle\int_0^1\!\!\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\).
3
Inner: \(\int_0^y e^{y^2}dx=y\,e^{y^2}\).
4
Outer: \(\int_0^1 y e^{y^2}dy=\big[\tfrac12 e^{y^2}\big]_0^1=\tfrac12(e-1).\)
The swap converts a non-elementary inner integral into a simple \(u\)-substitution. Recognising this is the whole point of the problem.
Limitations & pitfalls

Fubini requires the integrand be absolutely integrable on the region; for sign-changing improper integrands it can fail. Wrong limits after a swap is the dominant error — always re-derive from the sketch.

How it's asked on the exam

A near-certain integration problem. Yang favours the 'unswappable as written → swap' setup. Draw the region in the margin; partial credit follows the limits.

Change of Variables & Jacobian

When to use it

To simplify a region or integrand via a substitution (polar, or a custom \(u,v\)).

Procedure
  1. Choose the map \((x,y)=T(u,v)\); compute the Jacobian \(J=\det\dfrac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\).
  2. Replace \(dx\,dy=|J|\,du\,dv\).
  3. Transform the region's boundary into the \((u,v)\) plane.
  4. For polar: \(x=r\cos\theta,\,y=r\sin\theta,\;dx\,dy=r\,dr\,d\theta\).
Worked example — step through it
Example — polar for a disk
1
\(\displaystyle\iint_{x^2+y^2\le1}e^{-(x^2+y^2)}\,dA\). Use polar.
2
Integrand \(e^{-r^2}\), \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\); region \(0\le r\le1,\,0\le\theta\le2\pi\).
3
\(\int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^1 e^{-r^2}r\,dr\,d\theta=2\pi\big[-\tfrac12 e^{-r^2}\big]_0^1=2\pi\cdot\tfrac12(1-e^{-1}).\)
4
\(=\pi(1-e^{-1}).\)
The \(r\) from the Jacobian is exactly what makes \(e^{-r^2}r\) integrable — forgetting it is the classic polar mistake.
Example — custom \(u=xy,\,v=y/x\)
1
Region bounded by \(xy=1,xy=2,y=x,y=2x\) (first quadrant). Let \(u=xy,\,v=y/x\).
2
Then \(u\in[1,2],\,v\in[1,2]\) — a square. Need \(dA_{xy}=|J|\,du\,dv\).
3
\(\dfrac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}y&x\\-y/x^2&1/x\end{smallmatrix}\right)\), \(\det=\tfrac{y}{x}+\tfrac{y}{x}=\tfrac{2y}{x}=2v\).
4
So \(dA_{xy}=\dfrac{1}{|2v|}\,du\,dv\) — the reciprocal, since we computed \(\partial(u,v)/\partial(x,y)\).
This is the 2025 final's \(u=xy,\,v=y/x\) setup. Key subtlety: invert the Jacobian when you compute \(\partial(u,v)/\partial(x,y)\) instead of \(\partial(x,y)/\partial(u,v)\).
Limitations & pitfalls

The map must be a bijection on the region (watch sign of \(J\), take \(|J|\)). Mixing up which Jacobian you computed (forward vs inverse) flips the factor.

How it's asked on the exam

Yang's signature \(u=xy,\,v=y/x\) hyperbola-region substitution appeared on the 2025 final part (a). State whether you took \(\partial(x,y)/\partial(u,v)\) or its reciprocal.

Work / Line Integrals

When to use it

To compute work \(\int_\gamma F\cdot d\gamma\) of a field along a curve, or test path-independence.

Procedure
  1. Parametrise the curve \(\gamma(t),\,t\in[a,b]\); compute \(\gamma'(t)\).
  2. Substitute into \(\int_a^b F(\gamma(t))\cdot\gamma'(t)\,dt\).
  3. If \(F=\nabla\phi\) (conservative), shortcut: \(\int_\gamma F\cdot d\gamma=\phi(\text{end})-\phi(\text{start})\) — the Gradient Theorem.
  4. To find \(\phi\): integrate \(F_1\) in \(x\), differentiate w.r.t. \(y\) to fix the constant-of-integration function.
Worked example — step through it
Example — conservative shortcut
1
\(F=(2xy,\,x^2)\). Curl check: \(D_xF_2-D_yF_1=2x-2x=0\Rightarrow\) conservative.
2
Find \(\phi\): \(\int2xy\,dx=x^2y+g(y)\); \(\partial_y(x^2y+g)=x^2+g'(y)=x^2\Rightarrow g'=0\). So \(\phi=x^2y\).
3
Along any path from \((0,0)\) to \((1,2)\): \(\int_\gamma F\cdot d\gamma=\phi(1,2)-\phi(0,0)=2-0=2.\)
4
No parametrisation needed — path-independence does the work.
Always run the curl test first; if it's zero on a simply-connected domain, the potential shortcut saves enormous effort.
Limitations & pitfalls

The potential shortcut requires a simply-connected domain; a field can be curl-free but path-dependent around a hole (e.g. the vortex field). Otherwise you must parametrise.

How it's asked on the exam

Yang gives a 'messy \(F\), messy \(\gamma\)' and the intended move is to notice conservativeness (or to use Stokes). The last part of the 2025 final was an exact-differential line integral — recognise \(\phi\).

Flux / Surface Integrals

When to use it

To compute flux \(\iint_S F\cdot\mathbf n\,dS\) of a field through a surface.

Procedure
  1. Parametrise the surface \(\mathbf r(u,v)\) or write it as \(z=g(x,y)\).
  2. Normal: \(\mathbf n\,dS=\mathbf r_u\times\mathbf r_v\,du\,dv\) (or \((-g_x,-g_y,1)\,dA\) for a graph).
  3. Substitute \(F\) and dot with the normal; check the orientation matches what's asked.
  4. Integrate over the parameter region.
Worked example — step through it
Example — flux through a graph patch
1
\(F=(0,0,z)\), \(S:z=x^2+y^2\) over the unit disk, upward normal.
2
\(\mathbf n\,dS=(-2x,-2y,1)\,dA\). \(F\cdot\mathbf n\,dS=z\cdot1\,dA=(x^2+y^2)\,dA\).
3
Polar: \(\int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^1 r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=2\pi\cdot\tfrac14=\tfrac{\pi}{2}.\)
4
Flux \(=\pi/2\).
For a graph \(z=g\), the upward normal vector is \((-g_x,-g_y,1)\); the \(+1\) third component encodes the orientation.
Limitations & pitfalls

Orientation sign is the dominant error — a downward normal flips the answer. Closed surfaces are usually easier via the Divergence Theorem than direct flux.

How it's asked on the exam

Flux usually appears as a step toward the Divergence or Stokes theorem rather than alone. State your orientation choice explicitly.

Divergence Theorem (Green's / Gauss's)

When to use it

To convert flux through a CLOSED surface into a volume integral of \(\nabla\cdot F\) (3D Gauss) or a region integral (2D Green's).

Procedure
  1. Confirm the surface is closed and bounds a solid region \(V\) (outward orientation).
  2. Compute \(\nabla\cdot F\).
  3. Replace \(\displaystyle\oiint_{\partial V}F\cdot\mathbf n\,dS=\iiint_V \nabla\cdot F\,dV\).
  4. Evaluate the (usually simpler) volume integral, often in spherical/cylindrical coordinates.
Worked example — step through it
Example — flux out of the unit ball
1
\(F=(x,y,z)\), \(S=\) unit sphere, outward. Direct flux is doable but Gauss is faster.
2
\(\nabla\cdot F=1+1+1=3\).
3
\(\iiint_V 3\,dV=3\cdot\operatorname{Vol}(\text{ball})=3\cdot\tfrac43\pi(1)^3=4\pi.\)
4
Flux \(=4\pi\).
\(\nabla\cdot(x,y,z)=3\) and the ball volume is \(\tfrac43\pi\) — the product \(4\pi\) is a standard sanity value.
Limitations & pitfalls

Only for CLOSED surfaces; an open cap must be closed off (add a disk) and the added piece subtracted. \(F\) must be \(C^1\) inside — a singularity (e.g. at the origin for an inverse-square field) voids the theorem.

How it's asked on the exam

Green's 2D version (\(\oint F\cdot d\gamma=\iint(\partial_xF_2-\partial_yF_1)\,dA\)) is common on the planar problem; Gauss on the 3D one. Watch for singularities.

Curl Theorem (Stokes)

When to use it

To relate the circulation of \(F\) around a closed curve to the flux of \(\nabla\times F\) through any surface it bounds.

Procedure
  1. Identify the boundary curve \(\partial S\) and a surface \(S\) it bounds; orient by the right-hand rule.
  2. Compute \(\nabla\times F\).
  3. \(\displaystyle\oint_{\partial S}F\cdot d\gamma=\iint_S(\nabla\times F)\cdot\mathbf n\,dS\).
  4. Choose the EASIEST surface with that boundary (a flat disk often beats the original).
Worked example — step through it
Example — circulation via an easy cap
1
\(F=(-y,x,0)\), boundary = unit circle in the \(xy\)-plane, CCW.
2
\(\nabla\times F=(0,0,\,\partial_x x-\partial_y(-y))=(0,0,2)\).
3
Use the flat disk \(S\), normal \(\mathbf n=(0,0,1)\): \((\nabla\times F)\cdot\mathbf n=2\).
4
\(\iint_S 2\,dS=2\cdot\text{area}=2\pi.\) So the circulation \(=2\pi\).
The 2025 final's Stokes problem used a sphere∩plane circle as boundary and swapped to the flat disk — the 'rubber band slides to any cap' idea.
Limitations & pitfalls

All surfaces with the same boundary give the same answer ONLY if \(\nabla\times F\) is defined throughout (no singularities between them). Orientation of boundary vs normal must follow the right-hand rule or the sign flips.

How it's asked on the exam

Yang's hardest vector-calc problem: messy \(F\) and a hard-to-parametrise \(\gamma\) → the move is Stokes onto a flat disk. Compute curl carefully; that is where points are won.

Convergence Tests (Ch.16)

When to use it

To decide whether a number series \(\sum a_n\) converges, and whether absolutely or conditionally.

Procedure
  1. Quick divergence check: if \(a_n\not\to0\), the series diverges. Stop.
  2. Ratio test: \(L=\lim|a_{n+1}/a_n|\); \(L<1\) converges (absolutely), \(L>1\) diverges, \(L=1\) inconclusive.
  3. Root test for \(n\)-th powers; comparison / limit-comparison against a \(p\)-series \(\sum n^{-p}\) (converges iff \(p>1\)).
  4. Alternating series: if terms decrease to 0, it converges; check absolute convergence separately to classify conditional.
Worked example — step through it
Example — ratio test
1
\(\sum_{n\ge1}\dfrac{2^n}{n!}\). \(\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}=\dfrac{2^{n+1}/(n+1)!}{2^n/n!}=\dfrac{2}{n+1}\).
2
\(L=\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{2}{n+1}=0<1\).
3
Therefore the series converges absolutely.
4
(In fact \(\sum 2^n/n!=e^2-1\), but the test only needed \(L<1\).)
Factorials → ratio test almost always. The sum value is a bonus; convergence is the deliverable.
Prof. Yang's harmonic-series grouping proof
1
Claim: \(\sum 1/n=1+\tfrac12+\tfrac13+\cdots\) diverges.
2
Group: \((\tfrac13+\tfrac14)>2\cdot\tfrac14=\tfrac12\); \((\tfrac15+\cdots+\tfrac18)>4\cdot\tfrac18=\tfrac12\); each block \(>\tfrac12\).
3
Replace each group's terms by the smallest term in it — every group exceeds \(\tfrac12\).
4
Infinitely many half-units \(\Rightarrow\) partial sums \(\to\infty\). Diverges.
This is the exact grouping argument Prof. Yang presented. The trick — bound each group below by its smallest term — is the quotable technique.
Limitations & pitfalls

Ratio/root give NO information when \(L=1\) (e.g. all \(p\)-series). The \(n\)-th-term test can only prove divergence, never convergence.

How it's asked on the exam

One series problem on the final. Expect a ratio-test computation plus a conceptual part. Yang's harmonic grouping proof is fair game as 'show this diverges'.

Power Series & Taylor (Ch.17)

When to use it

To find the radius/interval of convergence of \(\sum c_n(x-a)^n\), or expand a function as a Taylor series.

Procedure
  1. Radius: \(R=1/\lim|c_{n+1}/c_n|\) (or root test). Series converges for \(|x-a|
  2. Check the two endpoints \(x=a\pm R\) separately with a number-series test.
  3. Taylor: \(f(x)=\sum \dfrac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n\). Use known expansions (\(e^x,\sin,\cos,\tfrac1{1-x}\)) and substitute.
  4. Differentiate/integrate term-by-term within \(R\) to derive new series.
Worked example — step through it
Example — interval of convergence
1
\(\sum_{n\ge1}\dfrac{(x-2)^n}{n\,3^n}\). Ratio: \(\left|\dfrac{(x-2)^{n+1}}{(n+1)3^{n+1}}\cdot\dfrac{n3^n}{(x-2)^n}\right|=\dfrac{n}{n+1}\cdot\dfrac{|x-2|}{3}\to\dfrac{|x-2|}{3}\).
2
Converges when \(|x-2|/3<1\Rightarrow|x-2|<3\Rightarrow R=3\), interval \((-1,5)\) before endpoints.
3
At \(x=5\): \(\sum 1/n\) — diverges. At \(x=-1\): \(\sum(-1)^n/n\) — converges (alternating).
4
Interval of convergence: \([-1,5)\).
Endpoints must each be tested with a number-series test — they often disagree, giving a half-open interval.
Limitations & pitfalls

Term-by-term differentiation/integration is valid only strictly inside \(R\). Forgetting to test endpoints loses the interval's boundary behaviour.

How it's asked on the exam

A Taylor/power-series part inside the single series problem. Yang stresses that Taylor is 'a series of functions' — connect it to pointwise convergence conceptually.

Pointwise Convergence & the Escape Examples (Ch.17)

When to use it

To explain why \(\lim\) and \(\int\) (or \(\tfrac{d}{dx}\)) cannot always be swapped — Yang's most-emphasised conceptual point.

Procedure
  1. Pointwise: \(F_n\to F\) if for every fixed \(x\), \(\lim_n F_n(x)=F(x)\).
  2. Warn: pointwise is 'nearsighted' — it checks one \(x\) at a time and misses global behaviour.
  3. It does NOT guarantee \(\lim\int F_n=\int\lim F_n\) nor \(\lim F_n'=(\lim F_n)'\).
  4. To show a swap fails, exhibit an escape example where the two sides give different numbers.
Worked example — step through it
Yang's 'horizontal escape'
1
\(F_n(x)=\) a fixed bump (area 1) shifted right by \(n\) — e.g. a normal curve centred at \(x=n\).
2
For every fixed \(x\): as \(n\to\infty\) the bump moves away, so \(F_n(x)\to0\). Thus \(F\equiv0\) pointwise.
3
\(\int\lim F_n=\int 0=0\), but \(\lim\int F_n=\lim 1=1\).
4
\(0\neq1\): limit and integral cannot be swapped.
The mass 'escapes to infinity' horizontally — pointwise sees 0 everywhere, the integral still sees the area 1.
Yang's 'vertical escape'
1
\(F_n=\) a triangle of area 1, stretched vertically by \(2^n\) and squeezed horizontally by \(2^n\).
2
For every fixed \(x\neq0\): eventually the spike avoids \(x\), so \(F_n(x)\to0\). Pointwise limit \(\equiv0\).
3
Yet each \(F_n\) has area 1, so \(\lim\int F_n=1\neq0=\int\lim F_n\).
4
Same failure mode, different geometry — the mass escapes upward.
Prof. Yang said these two examples are 'more important than the theorems' — they build the intuition for why uniform convergence is needed.
Limitations & pitfalls

Pointwise convergence is genuinely too weak for analysis swaps; the fix (uniform convergence / dominated convergence) is the next concept. Citing the theorem without an escape example misses Yang's intended answer.

How it's asked on the exam

Highly likely conceptual sub-part: 'give an example where \(\lim\int\neq\int\lim\)'. Reproduce one escape example with the two numbers (0 vs 1) — that is the full-credit answer.

Fourier Series Setup (Ch.18)

When to use it

To expand a periodic function \(f\) (period \(2\pi\)) into \(\tfrac{a_0}{2}+\sum(a_n\cos nx+b_n\sin nx)\).

Procedure
  1. \(a_0=\dfrac1\pi\displaystyle\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\,dx\).
  2. \(a_n=\dfrac1\pi\displaystyle\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\cos nx\,dx\).
  3. \(b_n=\dfrac1\pi\displaystyle\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\sin nx\,dx\).
  4. Use symmetry: even \(f\Rightarrow b_n=0\); odd \(f\Rightarrow a_n=0\) — halve the work.
Worked example — step through it
Example — square wave (odd)
1
\(f(x)=-1\) on \((-\pi,0)\), \(+1\) on \((0,\pi)\). Odd \(\Rightarrow a_n=0,\,a_0=0\).
2
\(b_n=\dfrac1\pi\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f\sin nx\,dx=\dfrac2\pi\int_0^\pi\sin nx\,dx\) (odd·odd = even).
3
\(=\dfrac2\pi\Big[-\dfrac{\cos nx}{n}\Big]_0^\pi=\dfrac{2}{n\pi}\big(1-\cos n\pi\big)=\dfrac{2}{n\pi}(1-(-1)^n).\)
4
So \(b_n=\dfrac{4}{n\pi}\) for odd \(n\), \(0\) for even \(n\): \(f(x)=\dfrac4\pi\sum_{k\ge0}\dfrac{\sin((2k+1)x)}{2k+1}.\)
Verified: \(1-(-1)^n=2\) for odd \(n\), \(0\) for even — giving the classic odd-harmonics-only square-wave series.
Limitations & pitfalls

The integrals assume \(f\) is integrable on the period; at jump discontinuities the series converges to the midpoint of the jump (Gibbs phenomenon near jumps). Using the wrong period scales every coefficient.

How it's asked on the exam

Part of the single series problem if Fourier appears. Exploit even/odd symmetry FIRST — it is the intended shortcut and saves an entire integral.
