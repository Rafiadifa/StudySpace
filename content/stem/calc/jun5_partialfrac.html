Change of Variables & the Jacobian
Calc A2 Final23 June 2026
Quick reference. For \((x,y)=T(u,v)\): \(\displaystyle\iint_R f\,dA_{xy}=\iint_S f\big(T(u,v)\big)\,\big|J\big|\,du\,dv\), where \(J=\dfrac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}=\det\begin{pmatrix}x_u&x_v\\y_u&y_v\end{pmatrix}\). Shortcut: \(\dfrac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}=\Big(\dfrac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}\Big)^{-1}\) — compute whichever is easier and invert. Choose \(u,v\) so the region becomes a rectangle (or the integrand simplifies).
★ Connected ProblemBend a curved region into a square (final P2 style)exam-style
Let \(R\) be the first-quadrant region bounded by \(xy=1,\ xy=2,\ y=x,\ y=2x\). Use \(u=xy,\ v=\dfrac{y}{x}\).
chains: choose \(u,v\) · map the region · Jacobian (invert) · evaluate
  1. (a)
    What is the image of \(R\) in the \((u,v)\)-plane?
    Solution\(xy\in[1,2]\Rightarrow u\in[1,2]\); \(y/x\in[1,2]\Rightarrow v\in[1,2]\). The curvy region becomes the square \([1,2]\times[1,2]\).
    unit-ish square \([1,2]^2\).
  2. (b)
    Compute the Jacobian \(\dfrac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\).
    SolutionEasier to do \(\dfrac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}=\det\begin{pmatrix}y&x\\-y/x^2&1/x\end{pmatrix}=\dfrac{y}{x}+\dfrac{y}{x}=\dfrac{2y}{x}=2v\). Invert: \(\dfrac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}=\dfrac1{2v}\).
    \(|J|=\dfrac1{2v}\).
  3. (c)
    Set up \(\displaystyle\iint_R dA\) (the area) in \((u,v)\).
    Solution\(\displaystyle\iint_R dA=\int_1^2\!\!\int_1^2\frac1{2v}\,du\,dv\).
    as shown.
  4. (d)
    Evaluate it.
    Solution\(\displaystyle\int_1^2 du\cdot\int_1^2\frac{dv}{2v}=1\cdot\tfrac12\ln v\big|_1^2=\tfrac12\ln2\).
    Area \(=\tfrac12\ln2\).
Problem 1 Just the Jacobian 8 pts
For the map \(x=u^2-v^2,\ y=2uv\), compute the Jacobian \(\dfrac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\).
Solution \(\dfrac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}=\det\begin{pmatrix}2u&-2v\\2v&2u\end{pmatrix}=4u^2+4v^2=4(u^2+v^2)\).
\(4(u^2+v^2)\).
This is the complex-squaring map \(z\mapsto z^2\); its area-scaling \(4|z|^2\) is exactly \(|2z|^2\).
Problem 2 Linear substitution simplifies the integrand 12 pts
Evaluate \(\displaystyle\iint_T e^{(x-y)/(x+y)}\,dA\), where \(T\) is the triangle with vertices \((0,0),(1,0),(0,1)\). Use \(u=x-y,\ v=x+y\).
Solution Invert: \(x=\tfrac{u+v}{2},\ y=\tfrac{v-u}{2}\Rightarrow\dfrac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}=\det\begin{pmatrix}1/2&1/2\\-1/2&1/2\end{pmatrix}=\tfrac12\). The triangle \(x,y\ge0,\ x+y\le1\) becomes \(v\in[0,1],\ -v\le u\le v\). \(\displaystyle\iint_T e^{(x-y)/(x+y)}dA=\tfrac12\int_0^1\!\!\int_{-v}^{v} e^{u/v}\,du\,dv=\tfrac12\int_0^1 v\big(e-e^{-1}\big)\,dv=\tfrac{e-e^{-1}}{4}\).
\(\dfrac{e-e^{-1}}{4}=\dfrac{e^2-1}{4e}\).
The exponent \(\tfrac{x-y}{x+y}=\tfrac uv\) is hopeless in \(x,y\) but trivial once you switch variables — the whole point of a substitution.
Problem 3 Polar IS a change of variables 10 pts
Show that \(x=r\cos\theta,\ y=r\sin\theta\) has Jacobian \(r\), then use it to evaluate \(\displaystyle\iint_{D} (x^2+y^2)\,dA\) over the unit disk \(D\).
Solution \(\dfrac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\theta)}=\det\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&-r\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&r\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}=r\cos^2\theta+r\sin^2\theta=r\). So \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\) and \(\displaystyle\iint_D(x^2+y^2)\,dA=\int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^1 r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=2\pi\cdot\tfrac14=\tfrac{\pi}{2}\).
\(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\).
Polar/cylindrical/spherical are just the change-of-variables formula with a known Jacobian — \(r\), \(r\), and \(\rho^2\sin\phi\) respectively.
