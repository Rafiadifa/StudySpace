Inverse Function Theorem
Calc A2 Final23 June 2026
Quick reference. A \(C^1\) map \(F:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^n\) is locally invertible near \(\mathbf x_0\) iff \(\det[DF(\mathbf x_0)]\neq0\). When invertible, the inverse's derivative is \([DF^{-1}](F(\mathbf x_0))=[DF(\mathbf x_0)]^{-1}\). In 1-D this is just \((f^{-1})'(y)=1/f'(x)\). The theorem is local and existential: it guarantees an inverse exists nearby, rarely in closed form.

Let \(F(x,y)=(x^2-y^2,\ 2xy)\).
chains: Jacobian · determinant · invertibility · derivative of the inverse
  1. (a)
    Compute \([DF]\).
    Solution\([DF]=\begin{pmatrix}2x&-2y\\2y&2x\end{pmatrix}\).
    as shown.
  2. (b)
    Where is \(F\) locally invertible?
    Solution\(\det[DF]=4x^2+4y^2=4(x^2+y^2)\), nonzero except at the origin.
    Invertible everywhere except \((0,0)\).
  3. (c)
    At \(P=(1,1)\), give \([DF^{-1}]\) at the image point.
    Solution\([DF(1,1)]=\begin{pmatrix}2&-2\\2&2\end{pmatrix}\), \(\det=8\); \([DF^{-1}]=[DF]^{-1}=\tfrac18\begin{pmatrix}2&2\\-2&2\end{pmatrix}\).
    \(=\tfrac14\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\-1&1\end{pmatrix}\).
  4. (d)
    What is that image point \(F(1,1)\)?
    Solution\(F(1,1)=(1-1,\ 2)=(0,2)\).
    \((0,2)\).
Problem 1 Where is the map invertible? 10 pts
For \(F(x,y)=(e^x\cos y,\;e^x\sin y)\) (polar-like map), compute \([DF]\), its determinant, and state where \(F\) is locally invertible.
Solution \([DF]=\begin{pmatrix}e^x\cos y & -e^x\sin y\\ e^x\sin y & e^x\cos y\end{pmatrix}\). \(\det=e^{2x}\cos^2y+e^{2x}\sin^2y=e^{2x}\).
\(\det=e^{2x}>0\) everywhere — \(F\) is locally invertible at every point.
Locally invertible everywhere, yet not globally one-to-one (it's \(2\pi\)-periodic in \(y\)). Local ≠ global.
Problem 2 Derivative of the inverse 10 pts
Let \(F(x,y)=(x+y,\;xy)\). At \((x,y)=(2,1)\), find \([DF]\), confirm invertibility, and give \([DF^{-1}]\) at the image point.
Solution \([DF]=\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ y & x\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 1 & 2\end{pmatrix}\) at \((2,1)\). \(\det=2-1=1\neq0\) ✓. Inverse of the derivative: \([DF^{-1}]=[DF]^{-1}=\dfrac{1}{1}\begin{pmatrix}2 & -1\\ -1 & 1\end{pmatrix}\).
\([DF^{-1}]=\begin{pmatrix}2 & -1\\ -1 & 1\end{pmatrix}\) at \(F(2,1)=(3,2)\).
Problem 3 Find the failure points 10 pts
For \(F(x,y)=(x^2-y^2,\;2xy)\) (the complex squaring map), where does the Inverse Function Theorem fail?
Solution \([DF]=\begin{pmatrix}2x & -2y\\ 2y & 2x\end{pmatrix}\), \(\det=4x^2+4y^2=4(x^2+y^2)\). This vanishes only at \((x,y)=(0,0)\).
IFT fails only at the origin (where \(\det=0\)); invertible everywhere else.
Setting the Jacobian determinant to zero and solving is the standard "where does it fail to be smooth/invertible?" move.
