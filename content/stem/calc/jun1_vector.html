Quick reference. A vector field \(F:U\to\mathbb R^n\) attaches an arrow to each point. A fixed point satisfies \(F(p)=\mathbf 0\). Near it, the field is locally linear: \(\mathbf x'=[DF(p)]\mathbf x\), and its type follows from the eigenvalues of the Jacobian — source (all \(\mathrm{Re}>0\)), sink (all \(\mathrm{Re}<0\)), saddle (mixed signs), spiral/orbit (complex).

Divergence \(\nabla\!\cdot\!F=\operatorname{trace}[DF]=D_1F_1+D_2F_2+\cdots\) (a scalar; source if \(>0\), sink if \(<0\), solenoidal if \(=0\)). Curl in 2D is the scalar \(\nabla\times F=D_1F_2-D_2F_1\); in 3D it is the vector \(\big(D_2F_3-D_3F_2,\;D_3F_1-D_1F_3,\;D_1F_2-D_2F_1\big)\). Two identities: \(\nabla\times(\nabla f)=\mathbf 0\) and \(\nabla\!\cdot\!(\nabla\times F)=0\).
Problem 1 Fixed Points & Local Linearization 12 pts
Consider the planar vector field
\[F(x,y)=\begin{pmatrix} x - xy \\ -y + xy \end{pmatrix}.\]
  1. (a)
    Find all fixed points of \(F\) (points where \(F=\mathbf 0\)). 3 pts
    Solution Set \(x-xy=x(1-y)=0\) and \(-y+xy=y(x-1)=0\). From the first: \(x=0\) or \(y=1\). From the second: \(y=0\) or \(x=1\). Pairing consistently: \((x,y)=(0,0)\) or \((1,1)\).
    Fixed points: \((0,0)\) and \((1,1)\).
  2. (b)
    Compute the Jacobian \([DF(x,y)]\) and explain why \(F\) is differentiable everywhere. 2 pts
    Solution \(F_1=x-xy,\;F_2=-y+xy\), so
    \([DF(x,y)]=\begin{pmatrix} 1-y & -x \\ y & -1+x \end{pmatrix}.\)
    Every entry is a polynomial, hence continuous everywhere, so \(F\) is differentiable on all of \(\mathbb R^2\).
  3. (c)
    Classify the fixed point \((0,0)\) using the eigenvalues of \([DF(0,0)]\). 4 pts
    Solution \([DF(0,0)]=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\). This is diagonal, so eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1=1\) and \(\lambda_2=-1\). One positive, one negative.
    Mixed-sign real eigenvalues \(\Rightarrow\) \((0,0)\) is a saddle.
    The flow repels along the \(x\)-axis (\(\lambda=1\)) and attracts along the \(y\)-axis (\(\lambda=-1\)).
  4. (d)
    Classify the fixed point \((1,1)\). (Find the eigenvalues of \([DF(1,1)]\) — they may be complex.) 3 pts
    Solution \([DF(1,1)]=\begin{pmatrix}1-1 & -1\\ 1 & -1+1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\). Characteristic equation: \(\lambda^2+1=0\Rightarrow\lambda=\pm i\).
    Purely imaginary eigenvalues \(\pm i\) \(\Rightarrow\) \((1,1)\) is an orbit center (closed circulating field lines).
    Re\((\lambda)=0\) means neither attracting nor repelling — the linearization predicts rotation around \((1,1)\).
Problem 2 Desert Wind — Divergence, Curl & the Cloud 12 pts
Standing in the desert, you feel the wind shift the dunes and shape the clouds — both are pictures of the same wind field's derivative. Model the wind velocity (in 3D) by
\[F(x,y,z)=\begin{pmatrix} x^2 - z \\ xy \\ -2xz + y \end{pmatrix}.\]
  1. (a)
    Compute the divergence \(\nabla\cdot F\). At the point \((1,2,1)\), will a cloud disperse, gather, or stay stable? 3 pts
    Solution \(\nabla\cdot F=D_1F_1+D_2F_2+D_3F_3=2x + x + (-2x)=x\). At \((1,2,1)\): \(\nabla\cdot F=1>0\).
    \(\nabla\cdot F=x\); at \((1,2,1)\) it equals \(+1>0\) — the wind pushes outward, so the cloud disperses.
  2. (b)
    Compute the curl \(\nabla\times F\) at the point \((1,2,1)\). 4 pts
    Solution With \(F_1=x^2-z,\;F_2=xy,\;F_3=-2xz+y\): \[\nabla\times F=\begin{pmatrix}D_2F_3-D_3F_2\\ D_3F_1-D_1F_3\\ D_1F_2-D_2F_1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1-0\\ -1-(-2z)\\ y-0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\ 2z-1\\ y\end{pmatrix}.\] At \((1,2,1)\):
    \(\nabla\times F(1,2,1)=\begin{pmatrix}1\\ 1\\ 2\end{pmatrix}.\)
    The wind spins locally around the axis \((1,1,2)\).
  3. (c)
    Is \(F\) a gradient field (i.e. \(F=\nabla\phi\) for some \(\phi\))? Justify using the curl. 2 pts
    Solution A gradient field is always curl-free: \(\nabla\times(\nabla\phi)=\mathbf 0\). Here \(\nabla\times F=(1,\,2z-1,\,y)\), which is not the zero vector (e.g. the first component is \(1\neq0\) everywhere).
    Since \(\nabla\times F\neq\mathbf 0\), \(F\) is not a gradient field.
  4. (d)
    Verify the identity \(\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=0\) for this field. 3 pts
    Solution From (b), \(\nabla\times F=(1,\;2z-1,\;y)\). Its divergence: \[\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=D_1(1)+D_2(2z-1)+D_3(y)=0+0+0=0.\]
    \(\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=0\) ✓ — confirming the "curls are solenoidal" identity (Clairaut's theorem in disguise).
