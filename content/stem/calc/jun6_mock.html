Problem 1 Single-Variable Techniques Drill 10 pts
Evaluate each integral. State the technique you used.
  1. (a)
    \(\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi} x\sin x\,dx\) 2 pts
    Solution — integration by parts Take \(u=x,\;dv=\sin x\,dx\Rightarrow du=dx,\;v=-\cos x\). \(\int x\sin x\,dx=-x\cos x+\int\cos x\,dx=-x\cos x+\sin x\). Evaluate \(0\to\pi\): \(\big[-x\cos x+\sin x\big]_0^\pi=(-\pi\cdot(-1)+0)-(0+0)=\pi\).
    \(\displaystyle\int_0^\pi x\sin x\,dx=\pi.\)
  2. (b)
    \(\displaystyle\int\frac{2x+1}{x^2+x-2}\,dx\) 3 pts
    Solution — recognise the derivative (fastest) Notice that \(\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^2+x-2)=2x+1\), exactly the numerator. With \(u=x^2+x-2,\,du=(2x+1)\,dx\): \[\int\frac{du}{u}=\ln|u|+C.\]
    \(\displaystyle\int\frac{2x+1}{x^2+x-2}\,dx=\ln|x^2+x-2|+C.\)
    Partial fractions also works (\(x^2+x-2=(x-1)(x+2)\) gives \(A=B=1\), summing to the same \(\ln|x-1|+\ln|x+2|=\ln|x^2+x-2|\)) but it's slower. Always check whether the numerator is the denominator's derivative before splitting.
  3. (c)
    \(\displaystyle\int_0^{\infty} 2x\,e^{-x^2}\,dx\) 2 pts
    Solution — substitution \(u=x^2\) \(u=x^2,\,du=2x\,dx\); limits \(x:0\to\infty\Rightarrow u:0\to\infty\). Integral becomes \(\int_0^\infty e^{-u}du=\big[-e^{-u}\big]_0^\infty=0-(-1)=1\).
    \(\displaystyle\int_0^\infty 2x\,e^{-x^2}\,dx=1.\)
  4. (d)
    Decide whether \(\displaystyle\int_1^{\infty}\frac{\ln x}{x^2}\,dx\) converges; if so, evaluate it. 3 pts
    Solution — improper, by parts Take \(u=\ln x,\,dv=x^{-2}dx\Rightarrow du=dx/x,\;v=-1/x\). \(\int\frac{\ln x}{x^2}dx=-\frac{\ln x}{x}+\int\frac{dx}{x^2}=-\frac{\ln x}{x}-\frac{1}{x}+C.\) Evaluate \(1\to b\) and take \(b\to\infty\): \(\big[-\tfrac{\ln x+1}{x}\big]_1^b=-\tfrac{\ln b+1}{b}-(-1)=1-\tfrac{\ln b+1}{b}\to1\) (by L'Hôpital, \(\ln b/b\to0\)).
    Converges to \(1\).
    Common error: dropping the integral term and writing only \(-\ln x/x\). The \(+1/x\) from the leftover integral matters — without it the limit is wrong.
Problem 2 Heat Sensor Grid — Order Swap 10 pts
A square heat-sensor grid records the temperature density across a region. To compute total heat over the triangular sub-region, you need to evaluate
\[I=\int_0^{1}\!\!\int_{\sqrt{y}}^{1}\sin(x^3)\,dx\,dy.\]
  1. (a)
    Sketch the region of integration and rewrite it as inequalities in \(x\) and \(y\). 2 pts
    Solution As written: \(0\le y\le 1\) and \(\sqrt y\le x\le 1\), i.e. \(y\le x^2\) with \(x\in[0,1]\).
    Region \(R=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\;0\le y\le x^2\}\) — the area under \(y=x^2\) over \([0,1]\).
  2. (b)
    Explain why the integral as written cannot be evaluated in closed form. 2 pts
    Solution The inner integral is \(\int\sin(x^3)\,dx\). There is no elementary antiderivative for \(\sin(x^3)\) (it is a Fresnel-type function), so iterating in this order is impossible by hand.
    The inner integral \(\int\sin(x^3)\,dx\) has no elementary form — the order must be swapped.
  3. (c)
    Swap the order of integration and evaluate \(I\) exactly. 5 pts
    Solution Swap to \(x\) outer: \(I=\int_0^1\!\!\int_0^{x^2}\sin(x^3)\,dy\,dx\). Inner: \(\int_0^{x^2}\sin(x^3)\,dy=x^2\sin(x^3)\) (sin is constant in \(y\)). Then \(I=\int_0^1 x^2\sin(x^3)\,dx\). Substitute \(u=x^3,\,du=3x^2\,dx\); \(x:0\to1\Rightarrow u:0\to1\): \[I=\frac{1}{3}\int_0^1\sin u\,du=\frac{1}{3}\big[-\cos u\big]_0^1=\frac{1-\cos 1}{3}.\]
    \(I=\dfrac{1-\cos 1}{3}\approx 0.153.\)
  4. (d)
    Why does this swap turn an impossible integral into a doable one? Identify the technical idea. 1 pt
    Solution After the swap, the inner integral has \(\sin(x^3)\) constant in \(y\), so the \(y\)-integration just multiplies by the strip width \(x^2\) — and \(x^2\) is exactly the derivative-of-\(x^3\) factor needed for a clean \(u=x^3\) substitution.
    The new inner integral is trivial, and the outer is a textbook \(u\)-substitution because \(x^2\,dx\) matches \(du/3\) for \(u=x^3\). Fubini's theorem guarantees both orders give the same value.
Problem 3 Change of Variables — Hyperbolic Region HARD 12 pts
Let \(R\) be the region in the first quadrant bounded by \(xy=1,\;xy=2,\;y=x,\;y=2x\). We will compute
\[I=\iint_R \frac{1}{xy}\,dA\] using the substitution \(u=xy,\;v=y/x\).
  1. (a)
    Find the range of \(u\) and \(v\) on \(R\). Sketch the image of \(R\) in the \((u,v)\)-plane. 2 pts
    Solution The boundary \(xy=1\) is \(u=1\); \(xy=2\) is \(u=2\); \(y=x\) is \(v=1\); \(y=2x\) is \(v=2\).
    \(u\in[1,2],\;v\in[1,2]\) — \(R\) maps to the unit square \([1,2]\times[1,2]\).
  2. (b)
    Compute the Jacobian \(\det\dfrac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}\) and use it to express \(dA_{xy}\) in terms of \(du\,dv\). 4 pts
    Solution \(\dfrac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}u_x&u_y\\v_x&v_y\end{smallmatrix}\right)=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}y&x\\-y/x^2&1/x\end{smallmatrix}\right).\) \(\det=\dfrac{y}{x}-x\cdot\left(-\dfrac{y}{x^2}\right)=\dfrac{y}{x}+\dfrac{y}{x}=\dfrac{2y}{x}=2v.\) Since we computed the forward Jacobian, \(dA_{xy}=\dfrac{1}{|J|}\,du\,dv=\dfrac{1}{2v}\,du\,dv\).
    \(dA_{xy}=\dfrac{1}{2v}\,du\,dv.\)
    Critical sign convention: \(dA_{xy}=|\det\partial(x,y)/\partial(u,v)|\,du\,dv\). Computing \(\partial(u,v)/\partial(x,y)\) gives the reciprocal — so divide by it. Flipping this is the #1 lost point on the 2025 final.
  3. (c)
    Rewrite the integrand \(1/(xy)\) in \((u,v)\) coordinates, then evaluate \(I\). 4 pts
    Solution \(\dfrac{1}{xy}=\dfrac{1}{u}\). So \[I=\int_1^2\!\!\int_1^2 \frac{1}{u}\cdot\frac{1}{2v}\,du\,dv=\frac{1}{2}\left(\int_1^2\frac{du}{u}\right)\!\left(\int_1^2\frac{dv}{v}\right)=\frac{1}{2}(\ln 2)(\ln 2).\]
    \(I=\dfrac{(\ln 2)^2}{2}\approx 0.240.\)
  4. (d)
    Why is the \(u=xy,\,v=y/x\) substitution natural for this region? What general principle does it illustrate? 2 pts
    Solution All four boundary curves are level sets of either \(xy\) or \(y/x\): \(xy=1,2\) (curves of constant \(u\)) and \(y=x,y=2x\) (rays of constant \(v\)). Picking new variables that are constant on the boundaries turns the curvy region into a rectangle.
    Principle: choose new variables that are constant along the boundary pieces — the image region becomes a rectangle in the new coordinates, killing the hard limits.
    This was the 2025 final's \(u=xy,\,v=y/x\) setup (Prof. Yang's signature problem). The technical content is identical to a polar substitution; only the choice of \((u,v)\) differs.
Problem 4 Conical Tank — Volume via Cylindrical Coordinates 8 pts
A water-storage tank is the solid region above the cone \(z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) and below the flat lid \(z=4\). Find the tank's volume.
  1. (a)
    Sketch the cross-section and write the region in cylindrical coordinates \((r,\theta,z)\). What is the range of \(r\)? 2 pts
    Solution In cylindrical, \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=r\), so the cone is \(z=r\). The cone meets the lid where \(r=4\), giving a top disk of radius 4.
    Region: \(\theta\in[0,2\pi],\;r\in[0,4],\;z\in[r,4].\)
  2. (b)
    Write the volume as a triple integral in cylindrical coordinates. Include the Jacobian factor. 2 pts
    Solution The cylindrical volume element is \(dV=r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta\).
    \(\displaystyle V=\int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^{4}\!\!\int_{r}^{4} r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta.\)
  3. (c)
    Evaluate the integral. 3 pts
    Solution Innermost (in \(z\)): \(\int_r^4 r\,dz=r(4-r)=4r-r^2\). Middle (in \(r\)): \(\int_0^4(4r-r^2)\,dr=\big[2r^2-\tfrac{r^3}{3}\big]_0^4=32-\tfrac{64}{3}=\tfrac{32}{3}\). Outermost (in \(\theta\)): \(\int_0^{2\pi}\tfrac{32}{3}\,d\theta=\tfrac{64\pi}{3}\).
    \(V=\dfrac{64\pi}{3}\approx 67.02\) cubic units.
  4. (d)
    Sanity-check by an alternative method: the tank is a cylinder of radius 4 height 4 minus a cone with the same base and height. 1 pt
    Solution Cylinder volume: \(\pi(4)^2\cdot 4=64\pi\). Cone volume: \(\tfrac{1}{3}\pi(4)^2\cdot 4=\tfrac{64\pi}{3}\). Tank \(=\) cylinder \(-\) cone \(=64\pi-\tfrac{64\pi}{3}=\tfrac{128\pi}{3}\dots\) — wait, that's bigger than our answer. The geometry: our solid is the region above the cone and below the lid — which IS exactly cylinder minus cone IF the cone has its tip at the origin pointing up. Re-check: that gives \(64\pi-\tfrac{64\pi}{3}=\tfrac{128\pi}{3}\). But our integral gave \(\tfrac{64\pi}{3}\). Which is right?
    Resolution: the solid \(\{r\le z\le 4\}\) is the inverted-cone-shaped region whose volume IS \(\tfrac{1}{3}\pi r_{\text{top}}^2 h=\tfrac{1}{3}\pi(4)^2(4)=\tfrac{64\pi}{3}\) — a single cone (not cylinder minus cone). The integral \(\tfrac{64\pi}{3}\) is correct.
    Trap to avoid: 'cylinder minus cone' describes the OUTSIDE of our region (the region above the lid extended down to the base disk). Always sketch — the solid above \(z=r\) and below \(z=4\) is itself a cone.
