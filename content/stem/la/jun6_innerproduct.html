Inner Product Spaces

Final · 25 Jun
Quick reference. An inner product \(\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle\) is (1) symmetric \(\langle u,v\rangle=\langle v,u\rangle\), (2) linear in the first slot, (3) positive-definite \(\langle v,v\rangle>0\) for \(v\neq0\). Norm \(\lVert v\rVert=\sqrt{\langle v,v\rangle}\). Angle \(\cos\theta=\frac{\langle u,v\rangle}{\lVert u\rVert\lVert v\rVert}\). Orthogonal \(\Leftrightarrow\langle u,v\rangle=0\). Cauchy–Schwarz: \(|\langle u,v\rangle|\le\lVert u\rVert\lVert v\rVert\).

In \(\mathbb R^3\), let \(a_1=(1,1,0),\ a_2=(1,0,1)\) and target \(b=(2,1,1)\).
chains: inner product · Gram–Schmidt · projection · least squares
  1. (a)
    Orthogonalise \(\{a_1,a_2\}\) by Gram–Schmidt.
    Solution\(u_1=(1,1,0)\); \(u_2=a_2-\tfrac{\langle a_2,u_1\rangle}{\langle u_1,u_1\rangle}u_1=(1,0,1)-\tfrac12(1,1,0)=(\tfrac12,-\tfrac12,1)\).
    orthogonal \(\{u_1,u_2\}\).
  2. (b)
    Project \(b\) onto \(u_1\).
    Solution\(\tfrac{\langle b,u_1\rangle}{\langle u_1,u_1\rangle}u_1=\tfrac{3}{2}(1,1,0)=(\tfrac32,\tfrac32,0)\).
    \((\tfrac32,\tfrac32,0)\).
  3. (c)
    Set up the least-squares normal equations for \(A\mathbf x=b\) with \(A=[a_1\ a_2]\).
    Solution\(A^TA=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix},\ A^Tb=\begin{pmatrix}3\\3\end{pmatrix}\); solve \(A^TA\hat{\mathbf x}=A^Tb\).
    \(\hat{\mathbf x}=(1,1)\).
  4. (d)
    What does \(A\hat{\mathbf x}\) represent?
    SolutionThe orthogonal projection of \(b\) onto \(\operatorname{Col}(A)\); the residual \(b-A\hat{\mathbf x}\perp\operatorname{Col}(A)\).
    projection of \(b\).
Problem 1Norm, angle, orthogonality9 pts
In \(\mathbb R^3\) with the dot product, \(u=(1,2,2),\ v=(2,0,-1)\).
  1. (a)
    \(\lVert u\rVert\) and \(\lVert v\rVert\). 3 pts
    Solution \(\lVert u\rVert=\sqrt{1+4+4}=3\), \(\lVert v\rVert=\sqrt{4+0+1}=\sqrt5\).
    \(3\) and \(\sqrt5\).
  2. (b)
    The angle between them. 3 pts
    Solution \(\langle u,v\rangle=2+0-2=0\). So \(\cos\theta=0\Rightarrow\theta=90^\circ\).
    Orthogonal — \(\theta=\tfrac\pi2\).
  3. (c)
    Are they orthogonal? 3 pts
    Solution Yes — \(\langle u,v\rangle=0\) is exactly the definition of orthogonality.
    Orthogonal.
Problem 2Verify the axioms10 pts
Is \(\langle u,v\rangle = u_1v_1 + 2u_2v_2\) a valid inner product on \(\mathbb R^2\)? Check all three axioms.
Solution Symmetric ✓ (swapping \(u,v\) gives the same). Linear in slot 1 ✓ (each term is). Positive-definite: \(\langle v,v\rangle=v_1^2+2v_2^2>0\) unless \(v=0\) ✓.
Yes — it's a weighted inner product (weights 1 and 2).
If a weight were \(\le0\) (e.g. \(-2u_2v_2\)) positive-definiteness fails and it's not an inner product.
Problem 3Function inner product10 pts
On \(C[0,1]\) with \(\langle f,g\rangle=\int_0^1 f g\,dx\), compute \(\langle x, x^2\rangle\) and \(\lVert x\rVert\).
Solution \(\langle x,x^2\rangle=\int_0^1 x^3\,dx=\tfrac14\). \(\lVert x\rVert^2=\int_0^1 x^2\,dx=\tfrac13\Rightarrow\lVert x\rVert=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt3}\).
\(\langle x,x^2\rangle=\tfrac14\), \(\lVert x\rVert=1/\sqrt3\).
The same inner-product machinery works on function spaces — integrals replace sums.
Problem 4Cauchy–Schwarz8 pts
Verify Cauchy–Schwarz for \(u=(1,2),\ v=(3,1)\) under the dot product.
Solution \(|\langle u,v\rangle|=|3+2|=5\). \(\lVert u\rVert\lVert v\rVert=\sqrt5\cdot\sqrt{10}=\sqrt{50}\approx7.07\). Indeed \(5\le7.07\) ✓.
\(5\le\sqrt{50}\) — inequality holds (strict, since \(u,v\) aren't parallel).
Equality in Cauchy–Schwarz holds iff \(u,v\) are linearly dependent.
