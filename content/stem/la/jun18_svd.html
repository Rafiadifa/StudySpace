Singular Value Decomposition
Lin Alg Final25 June 2026
Quick reference. \(A=U\Sigma V^T\). Recipe: \(V\)= orthonormal eigenvectors of \(A^TA\); singular values \(\sigma_i=\sqrt{\lambda_i}\) (the diagonal of \(\Sigma\)); \(U\) columns \(u_i=\frac{1}{\sigma_i}Av_i\). Works for any \(m\times n\) matrix.
★ Connected ProblemEqual singular values ⇒ a circleexam-style
Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\).
chains: \(A^{\mathsf T}A\) · singular values · SVD · image of the unit circle
  1. (a)
    Compute \(A^{\mathsf T}A\) and the singular values.
    Solution\(A^{\mathsf T}A=\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0&2\end{pmatrix}=2I\), eigenvalues \(2,2\Rightarrow\sigma_1=\sigma_2=\sqrt2\).
    \(\sigma=\sqrt2,\ \sqrt2\).
  2. (b)
    Write an SVD \(A=U\Sigma V^{\mathsf T}\).
    Solution\(A^{\mathsf T}A=2I\) ⇒ any orthonormal \(V\) works; take \(V=I\), \(\Sigma=\sqrt2\,I\), \(U=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}A=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\) (orthogonal).
    \(U=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}A,\ \Sigma=\sqrt2 I,\ V=I\).
  3. (c)
    What is the image of the unit circle under \(A\)?
    SolutionBoth singular values are equal, so the "ellipse" is actually a circle of radius \(\sqrt2\) — no preferred axis. (Contrast the ★ Challenge below, where unequal \(\sigma\) give a genuine ellipse.)
    circle, radius \(\sqrt2\).
  4. (d)
    Explain this from the structure of \(A\).
    Solution\(A=\sqrt2\,U\) with \(U\) orthogonal, so \(A\) is just an orthogonal map scaled by \(\sqrt2\); orthogonal maps preserve shape, so a circle stays a (bigger) circle. \(|\det A|=2=\sigma_1\sigma_2\).
    scaled orthogonal map.
Problem 1Rank-1 SVD15 pts
\(A=\begin{pmatrix}3&0\\4&0\end{pmatrix}\). Find the SVD.
Solution\(A^TA=\begin{pmatrix}25&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix}\), eigenvalues \(25,0\Rightarrow\sigma_1=5,\sigma_2=0\). \(v_1=(1,0),v_2=(0,1)\). \(u_1=\tfrac15A v_1=\tfrac15(3,4)\); complete \(u_2=(-4/5,3/5)\).
\(U=\tfrac15\begin{pmatrix}3&-4\\4&3\end{pmatrix},\ \Sigma=\begin{pmatrix}5&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix},\ V=I\).
Rank 1 ⇒ one nonzero singular value. \(u_1\) is the unit vector along the column \((3,4)\).
Problem 2Why \(A^TA\)?8 pts
Why are singular values square roots of eigenvalues of \(A^TA\)?
Solution\(A^TA\) is symmetric positive-semidefinite, so it has real \(\ge0\) eigenvalues. \(\lVert Av\rVert^2=v^TA^TAv=\lambda\) for a unit eigenvector, so \(\sigma=\lVert Av\rVert=\sqrt\lambda\).
\(\sigma_i=\sqrt{\lambda_i(A^TA)}\).
★ ChallengeExam-level · SVD as the geometry of an ellipsepast-final level
Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}2&-1\\2&1\end{pmatrix}\). (Non-symmetric, so the SVD is genuinely different from an eigendecomposition.)
chains: \(A^{\mathsf T}A\) · singular values · full \(U\Sigma V^{\mathsf T}\) · ellipse image · norm & area
  1. (a)
    Form \(A^{\mathsf T}A\) and read off the singular values.
    Solution\(A^{\mathsf T}A=\begin{pmatrix}8&0\\0&2\end{pmatrix}\) (already diagonal), eigenvalues \(8,2\Rightarrow\sigma_1=2\sqrt2,\ \sigma_2=\sqrt2\).
    \(\sigma=2\sqrt2,\ \sqrt2\).
  2. (b)
    Write the full SVD \(A=U\Sigma V^{\mathsf T}\).
    Solution\(A^{\mathsf T}A\) diagonal ⇒ \(V=I\). \(u_i=\tfrac1{\sigma_i}Av_i\): \(u_1=\tfrac1{2\sqrt2}(2,2)=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(1,1)\), \(u_2=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(-1,1)\).
    \(U=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}1&-1\\1&1\end{pmatrix},\ \Sigma=\begin{pmatrix}2\sqrt2&0\\0&\sqrt2\end{pmatrix},\ V=I\).
  3. (c)
    The image of the unit circle under \(A\) is an ellipse — give the semi-axis lengths and directions.
    SolutionSemi-axes have length \(\sigma_i\) along \(u_i\): length \(2\sqrt2\) along \(\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(1,1)\), and \(\sqrt2\) along \(\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(-1,1)\).
    \(2\sqrt2\) along \((1,1)\); \(\sqrt2\) along \((-1,1)\).
  4. (d)
    Give \(\lVert A\rVert_2\) (the largest stretch) and the area-scaling factor; relate the latter to \(\det A\).
    Solution\(\lVert A\rVert_2=\sigma_{\max}=2\sqrt2\). Area scales by \(\sigma_1\sigma_2=2\sqrt2\cdot\sqrt2=4\), and indeed \(|\det A|=|2\cdot1-(-1)\cdot2|=4\).
    \(\lVert A\rVert_2=2\sqrt2\); area \(\times4=|\det A|\).
