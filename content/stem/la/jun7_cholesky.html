Cholesky Decomposition

Final · 25 Jun
Quick reference. A symmetric matrix \(G\) is positive-definite iff all leading principal minors are \(>0\) (Sylvester's criterion). Then \(G=LL^{T}\) with \(L\) lower-triangular, positive diagonal. Entry formulas: \(L_{jj}=\sqrt{G_{jj}-\sum_{kj\).
★ Connected ProblemSymmetric matrix: spectral, definiteness, quadratic formexam-style
Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix}\) and \(Q(\mathbf x)=\mathbf x^TA\mathbf x\).
chains: spectral decomposition · definiteness · quadratic form · application
  1. (a)
    Orthogonally diagonalise \(A=Q\Lambda Q^T\).
    Solution\(\lambda=3,(1,1);\ 1,(1,-1)\); \(Q=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix},\Lambda=\operatorname{diag}(3,1)\).
    as shown.
  2. (b)
    Classify the quadratic form \(Q\).
    SolutionEigenvalues \(3,1>0\Rightarrow\) positive definite.
    Positive definite.
  3. (c)
    Give the Cholesky factor \(A=LL^T\).
    Solution\(L_{11}=\sqrt2,\ L_{21}=\tfrac1{\sqrt2},\ L_{22}=\sqrt{2-\tfrac12}=\sqrt{\tfrac32}\).
    \(L=\begin{pmatrix}\sqrt2&0\\1/\sqrt2&\sqrt{3/2}\end{pmatrix}\).
  4. (d)
    What are the singular values of \(A\)?
    Solution\(A\) symmetric positive definite ⇒ singular values \(=\) eigenvalues \(=3,1\).
    \(\sigma=3,1\).
Problem 1Check positive-definiteness8 pts
Is \(G=\begin{pmatrix}4&2\\2&3\end{pmatrix}\) positive-definite? Use leading minors.
Solution \(1\times1\) minor: \(4>0\) ✓. \(2\times2\) minor: \(\det=12-4=8>0\) ✓.
Both minors positive ⇒ positive-definite; Cholesky exists.
Problem 22×2 factorisation10 pts
Find \(L\) such that \(\begin{pmatrix}4&2\\2&3\end{pmatrix}=LL^{T}\).
Solution \(L_{11}=\sqrt4=2\). \(L_{21}=G_{21}/L_{11}=2/2=1\). \(L_{22}=\sqrt{G_{22}-L_{21}^2}=\sqrt{3-1}=\sqrt2\).
\(L=\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\1&\sqrt2\end{pmatrix}\). Check: \(LL^{T}=\begin{pmatrix}4&2\\2&3\end{pmatrix}\) ✓.
Problem 33×3 factorisation12 pts
Factor \(G=\begin{pmatrix}4&2&-2\\2&5&-3\\-2&-3&6\end{pmatrix}=LL^{T}\).
Solution Column 1: \(L_{11}=\sqrt4=2\), \(L_{21}=2/2=1\), \(L_{31}=-2/2=-1\).
Column 2: \(L_{22}=\sqrt{5-1^2}=2\), \(L_{32}=\frac{1}{2}(-3-(-1)(1))=\frac{1}{2}(-2)=-1\).
Column 3: \(L_{33}=\sqrt{6-((-1)^2+(-1)^2)}=\sqrt{6-2}=2\).
\(L=\begin{pmatrix}2&0&0\\1&2&0\\-1&-1&2\end{pmatrix}\).
Work column by column, top to bottom. Each diagonal subtracts the squares already placed in that row; each below-diagonal subtracts the dot of the two partial rows.
Problem 4When it fails8 pts
Why does Cholesky fail for \(\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&1\end{pmatrix}\)? Show where the algorithm breaks.
Solution \(L_{11}=1\), \(L_{21}=2\). Then \(L_{22}=\sqrt{1-2^2}=\sqrt{-3}\) — a negative under the root. The \(2\times2\) minor is \(1-4=-3<0\), so the matrix is not positive-definite.
Fails: \(\det=-3<0\) ⇒ not positive-definite ⇒ no real Cholesky factor.
A negative under the diagonal square root is exactly the algorithm detecting non-positive-definiteness.
