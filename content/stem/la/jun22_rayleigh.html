Rayleigh Quotient
Lin Alg Final25 June 2026
Quick reference. For a symmetric matrix \(A\), the Rayleigh quotient is \(R(\mathbf x)=\dfrac{\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}A\mathbf x}{\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}\mathbf x}\) (\(\mathbf x\neq\mathbf 0\)). Then \(\lambda_{\min}\le R(\mathbf x)\le\lambda_{\max}\): the max equals \(\lambda_{\max}\) (at its eigenvector), the min equals \(\lambda_{\min}\) (at its eigenvector), and \(R(\mathbf v_i)=\lambda_i\). Maximising \(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}A\mathbf x\) subject to \(\lVert\mathbf x\rVert=1\) gives the eigenproblem \(A\mathbf x=\lambda\mathbf x\). For general \(A\), \(\max \lVert A\mathbf x\rVert/\lVert\mathbf x\rVert=\sigma_{\max}=\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(A^{\mathsf T}A)}\) — the Rayleigh quotient of \(A^{\mathsf T}A\) gives squared singular values.

Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}5&2\\2&2\end{pmatrix}\) (symmetric) and \(R(\mathbf x)=\dfrac{\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}A\mathbf x}{\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}\mathbf x}\).
chains: evaluate R · eigenvalues · max/min via Rayleigh · definiteness
  1. (a)
    Compute \(R(\mathbf x)\) for \(\mathbf x=(1,0)\).
    Solution\(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}A\mathbf x=(1,0)\!\cdot\!A(1,0)=(1,0)\!\cdot\!(5,2)=5\); \(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}\mathbf x=1\). So \(R=5\).
    \(R(1,0)=5\).
  2. (b)
    Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors.
    Solution\(\operatorname{tr}A=7,\ \det A=6\Rightarrow\lambda^2-7\lambda+6=(\lambda-6)(\lambda-1)\). \(\lambda=6:(2,1);\ \lambda=1:(1,-2)\).
    \(\lambda=6,(2,1);\ 1,(1,-2)\).
  3. (c)
    What are the max and min of \(R\) over \(\mathbf x\neq\mathbf 0\), and where are they attained?
    Solution\(\max R=\lambda_{\max}=6\) at \(\mathbf x=(2,1)\); \(\min R=\lambda_{\min}=1\) at \(\mathbf x=(1,-2)\). Check: \(R(2,1)=\tfrac{(2,1)\cdot(12,6)}{5}=\tfrac{30}{5}=6\), \(R(1,-2)=\tfrac{(1,-2)\cdot(1,-2)}{5}=\tfrac{5}{5}=1\) — and \(1\le5\le6\) matches part (a).
    max \(6\) at \((2,1)\); min \(1\) at \((1,-2)\).
  4. (d)
    Is \(A\) positive definite? Justify via the Rayleigh quotient.
    Solution\(R(\mathbf x)\ge\lambda_{\min}=1>0\) for every \(\mathbf x\neq\mathbf 0\), i.e. \(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}A\mathbf x>0\) always — so \(A\) is positive definite. (Equivalently, both eigenvalues are \(>0\).)
    PD, since \(\min R=1>0\).
Problem 1Evaluate & bound
  1. (a)
    \(A=\begin{pmatrix}4&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf x=(1,1)\). Compute \(R(\mathbf x)\). 3
    Solution\(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}A\mathbf x=(1,1)\cdot(4,1)=5\); \(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}\mathbf x=2\). \(R=\tfrac52\).
  2. (b)
    Give \(\max R\) and \(\min R\) and the \(\mathbf x\) attaining each. 3
    SolutionDiagonal, so eigenvalues \(4\) (at \((1,0)\)) and \(1\) (at \((0,1)\)). \(\max R=4,\ \min R=1\).
  3. (c)
    Verify your value in (a) obeys \(\lambda_{\min}\le R\le\lambda_{\max}\). 2
    Solution\(1\le\tfrac52\le4\) ✓.
Problem 2Constrained optimisation
  1. (a)
    For \(A=\begin{pmatrix}3&0\\0&5\end{pmatrix}\), maximise \(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}A\mathbf x\) subject to \(\lVert\mathbf x\rVert=1\). 3
    SolutionOn \(\lVert\mathbf x\rVert=1\), \(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}A\mathbf x=R(\mathbf x)\le\lambda_{\max}=5\), attained at \(\mathbf x=(0,1)\). (Min is \(3\) at \((1,0)\).)
  2. (b)
    Show the Lagrange condition reduces to an eigenproblem. 3
    SolutionMaximise \(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}A\mathbf x\) s.t. \(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}\mathbf x=1\): \(\nabla(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}A\mathbf x)=2A\mathbf x=\lambda\,\nabla(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}\mathbf x)=2\lambda\mathbf x\Rightarrow A\mathbf x=\lambda\mathbf x\). Critical points are eigenvectors; the multiplier \(\lambda\) is the eigenvalue (= the value of \(R\) there).
Problem 3Definiteness via Rayleigh
  1. (a)
    Classify \(A=\begin{pmatrix}2&-1\\-1&2\end{pmatrix}\) using the Rayleigh range. 3
    Solution\(\lambda=1,3\) (\(\operatorname{tr}4,\det3\)). \(R(\mathbf x)\ge\lambda_{\min}=1>0\Rightarrow\) positive definite.
  2. (b)
    Classify \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&1\end{pmatrix}\) and find an \(\mathbf x\) with \(R(\mathbf x)<0\). 3
    Solution\(\lambda=3,-1\) (\(\operatorname{tr}2,\det-3\)). \(\lambda_{\min}=-1<0\Rightarrow\) indefinite. E.g. \(\mathbf x=(1,-1)\): \(A\mathbf x=(-1,1)\), \(R=\tfrac{(1,-1)\cdot(-1,1)}{2}=\tfrac{-2}{2}=-1<0\).
★ ChallengeRayleigh of \(A^{\mathsf T}A\) = singular valuespast-final level
Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{pmatrix}\) (not symmetric).
chains: \(\lVert A\mathbf x\rVert^2/\lVert\mathbf x\rVert^2\) · Rayleigh of \(A^{\mathsf T}A\) · largest singular value
  1. (a)
    Express \(\dfrac{\lVert A\mathbf x\rVert^2}{\lVert\mathbf x\rVert^2}\) as a Rayleigh quotient.
    Solution\(\lVert A\mathbf x\rVert^2=(A\mathbf x)^{\mathsf T}(A\mathbf x)=\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}(A^{\mathsf T}A)\mathbf x\), so the ratio is \(R_{A^{\mathsf T}A}(\mathbf x)\), the Rayleigh quotient of the symmetric matrix \(A^{\mathsf T}A\).
    \(=R_{A^{\mathsf T}A}(\mathbf x)\).
  2. (b)
    Compute \(A^{\mathsf T}A\) and its eigenvalues.
    Solution\(A^{\mathsf T}A=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix}\), \(\operatorname{tr}=3,\ \det=1\Rightarrow\lambda=\tfrac{3\pm\sqrt5}{2}\).
    \(\lambda=\tfrac{3\pm\sqrt5}{2}\).
  3. (c)
    What is the largest value of \(\lVert A\mathbf x\rVert/\lVert\mathbf x\rVert\), and what is it called?
    Solution\(\max \lVert A\mathbf x\rVert/\lVert\mathbf x\rVert=\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(A^{\mathsf T}A)}=\sqrt{\tfrac{3+\sqrt5}{2}}=\tfrac{1+\sqrt5}{2}\) (the golden ratio) — the largest singular value \(\sigma_{\max}\).
    \(\sigma_{\max}=\tfrac{1+\sqrt5}{2}\).
