Quick reference. Eigenvalues solve \(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\). For each \(\lambda\), the eigenvectors span \(\mathrm{Nul}(A-\lambda I)\). For \(2\times2\): characteristic poly \(\lambda^2-(\mathrm{tr}A)\lambda+\det A=0\).
★ Connected ProblemChar poly → eigen → diagonalise → powerexam-style
Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}\).
chains: characteristic polynomial · eigenvectors · diagonalisation · matrix power
  1. (a)
    Find the characteristic polynomial and eigenvalues.
    Solution\(\lambda^2-6\lambda+8=0\Rightarrow\lambda=4,2\).
    \(\lambda=4,2\).
  2. (b)
    Find eigenvectors and write \(A=PDP^{-1}\).
    Solution\(\lambda=4:(1,1);\ \lambda=2:(1,-1)\). \(P=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix},D=\operatorname{diag}(4,2)\).
    \(A=PDP^{-1}\).
  3. (c)
    Use it to compute \(A^n\) in closed form.
    Solution\(A^n=PD^nP^{-1}=\tfrac12\begin{pmatrix}4^n+2^n&4^n-2^n\\4^n-2^n&4^n+2^n\end{pmatrix}\).
    as shown.
  4. (d)
    Is \(A\) positive definite? Why?
    SolutionSymmetric with eigenvalues \(4,2>0\Rightarrow\) yes.
    Positive definite.

\(A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix}\). Find eigenvalues and eigenvectors.
Solution\(\lambda^2-4\lambda+3=0\Rightarrow\lambda=3,1\). For \(\lambda=3\): \((A-3I)=\begin{pmatrix}-1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\Rightarrow v=(1,1)\). For \(\lambda=1\): \(v=(1,-1)\).
\(\lambda=3,(1,1)\); \(\lambda=1,(1,-1)\).

Solution\(\lambda^2-7\lambda+10=0\Rightarrow\lambda=5,2\). \(\lambda=5\): \((A-5I)=\begin{pmatrix}-1&1\\2&-2\end{pmatrix}\Rightarrow v=(1,1)\). \(\lambda=2\): \(\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\2&1\end{pmatrix}\Rightarrow v=(1,-2)\).
\(\lambda=5,(1,1)\); \(\lambda=2,(1,-2)\).

\(\lambda=3,(1,0)\); \(\lambda=-1,(0,1)\).
