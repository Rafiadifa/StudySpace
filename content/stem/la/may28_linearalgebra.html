15:20–16:55 Change of Basis & Matrix Inverse ~95 MIN · EZ Ch.5.4 · 3 matrices to build
Problem 1  ·  12 points

A music synthesiser stores every tone as a quadratic envelope living in \(P_2\) (polynomials of degree \(\le 2\)). The "attack" engineer prefers the basis \(\mathcal{B}=\big(1,\;x+1,\;(x+1)^2\big)\), while the "release" engineer insists on \(\mathcal{C}=\big(1,\;x-1,\;(x-1)^2\big)\). They keep arguing because the same tone gets two different coordinate vectors. Let's settle it once and for all.

  1. Find the change-of-coordinate matrix \(I_{\mathcal{C}\leftarrow\mathcal{B}}\) (the matrix of the identity map taking \(\mathcal B\)-coordinates to \(\mathcal C\)-coordinates). [2 pts]
  2. Find \(I_{\mathcal{B}\leftarrow\mathcal{C}}\) by computing the inverse of your matrix in (a) by hand, and verify the product is \(I\). [3 pts]
  3. The tone \(w=2x^2-3x+5\) comes in. Give its coordinate vector in \(\mathcal B\) and in \(\mathcal C\), using a matrix from above to convert between them. [2 pts]
  4. Let \(D:P_2\to P_2\) be differentiation. Write the matrix \([D]_{\mathcal B}\), then use a change of basis to obtain \([D]_{\mathcal C}\). [3 pts]
  5. Are \([D]_{\mathcal B}\) and \([D]_{\mathcal C}\) similar? Name three quantities that must agree for any two matrices of the same map, and state the eigenvalues of \(D\). [2 pts]
Reveal answer
(a) Columns of \(I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B}\) are the old basis vectors (from \(\mathcal B\)) written in \(\mathcal C\)-coordinates: \(1=1\); \(x+1=2\cdot1+1\cdot(x-1)\); \((x+1)^2=4\cdot1+4(x-1)+(x-1)^2\). So \[I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B}=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&4\\0&1&4\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}.\] (b) Inverting the unit upper-triangular matrix (back-substitution / Gauss–Jordan): \[I_{\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal C}=\begin{pmatrix}1&-2&4\\0&1&-4\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}.\] Check: \(\begin{pmatrix}1&2&4\\0&1&4\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}\!\begin{pmatrix}1&-2&4\\0&1&-4\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}=I\). ✓ (Equivalently its columns are the \(\mathcal C\) vectors in \(\mathcal B\)-coordinates — same answer, good cross-check.) (c) Matching coefficients, \(w=10\cdot1-7(x+1)+2(x+1)^2\), so \(w_{\mathcal B}=(10,-7,2)^{\mathsf T}\). Then \(w_{\mathcal C}=I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B}\,w_{\mathcal B} =(10-14+8,\,-7+8,\,2)^{\mathsf T}=(4,1,2)^{\mathsf T}\). Direct expansion \(w=4+1\cdot(x-1)+2(x-1)^2\) confirms it. ✓ (d) \(D(1)=0,\;D(x+1)=1,\;D((x+1)^2)=2(x+1)\), so \[[D]_{\mathcal B}=\begin{pmatrix}0&1&0\\0&0&2\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad [D]_{\mathcal C}=I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B}\,[D]_{\mathcal B}\,I_{\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal C} =\begin{pmatrix}0&1&0\\0&0&2\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix}.\] The matrices are identical — because both bases are "shifted power" bases, differentiation looks the same in each. (Directly: \(D(x-1)=1,\;D((x-1)^2)=2(x-1)\).) (e) Yes — they are similar via \(P=I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B}\): \([D]_{\mathcal C}=P[D]_{\mathcal B}P^{-1}\) (here they even coincide). Any two matrices of the same map share determinant, trace, and characteristic polynomial (hence eigenvalues and rank). The characteristic polynomial is \(\lambda^3\), so every eigenvalue of \(D\) is \(0\): \(D\) is nilpotent with \(D^3=0\) on \(P_2\).
Problem 2  ·  10 points

The cafeteria sells three mystery "combos." Combo \(i\) delivers a fixed amount of protein, carbs and fat, encoded as the columns of a matrix. To plan meals you must be able to invert the recipe — given a nutrition target, how many of each combo? Consider \[M_A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1&1\\1&2&1\\1&1&2\end{pmatrix},\qquad M_B=\begin{pmatrix}1&1&1\\0&1&1\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix},\] \[M_C=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\0&1&4\\5&6&0\end{pmatrix},\qquad M_D=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\2&4&6\\1&0&1\end{pmatrix}.\]

  1. Compute \(M_A^{-1}\) and \(M_B^{-1}\). [2 pts]
  2. Compute \(M_C^{-1}\) (Gauss–Jordan or adjugate — show the determinant). [3 pts]
  3. Using your \(M_C^{-1}\), solve \(M_C\mathbf x=\mathbf b\) for the two targets \(\mathbf b_1=(1,0,0)^{\mathsf T}\) and \(\mathbf b_2=(6,4,11)^{\mathsf T}\). (This is the payoff of computing an inverse: every new target is just one matrix–vector product.) [2 pts]
  4. Show \(M_D\) has no inverse. Find \(\operatorname{rank}(M_D)\) and \(\dim\operatorname{Ker}(M_D)\), and say what this means for solving \(M_D\mathbf x=\mathbf b\). [3 pts]
Reveal answer
(a) \(M_A=I+J\) where \(J\) is all-ones; since \(J^2=3J\), \((I+J)^{-1}=I-\tfrac14J\): \[M_A^{-1}=\tfrac14\begin{pmatrix}3&-1&-1\\-1&3&-1\\-1&-1&3\end{pmatrix}.\] \(M_B\) is unit upper-triangular, so \(M_B^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}1&-1&0\\0&1&-1\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}\). (b) \(\det M_C = 1(1\cdot0-4\cdot6)-2(0\cdot0-4\cdot5)+3(0\cdot6-1\cdot5)=-24+40-15=1\). With \(\det=1\) the inverse equals the adjugate: \[M_C^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}-24&18&5\\20&-15&-4\\-5&4&1\end{pmatrix}.\] (Spot-check: row \(1\) of \(M_C\) times column \(1\) of \(M_C^{-1}\) gives \(-24+40-15=1\). ✓) (c) \(\mathbf x=M_C^{-1}\mathbf b\). For \(\mathbf b_1=(1,0,0)^{\mathsf T}\): \(\mathbf x_1=(-24,20,-5)^{\mathsf T}\) (the first column of \(M_C^{-1}\)). For \(\mathbf b_2=(6,4,11)^{\mathsf T}\): \(\mathbf x_2=(-24\cdot6+18\cdot4+5\cdot11,\;20\cdot6-15\cdot4-4\cdot11,\;-5\cdot6+4\cdot4+11)^{\mathsf T} =(-144+72+55,\;120-60-44,\;-30+16+11)^{\mathsf T}=(-17,16,-3)^{\mathsf T}\). Note the efficiency: five more targets would be five more products, no re-elimination. (d) Row \(2 = 2\times\) row \(1\), so \(\det M_D=0\) and \(M_D\) is not invertible. Row-reducing leaves two independent rows \(\Rightarrow\) \(\operatorname{rank}(M_D)=2\), and by rank-nullity \(\dim\operatorname{Ker}(M_D)=3-2=1\). Consequently \(M_D\mathbf x=\mathbf b\) is either inconsistent or has infinitely many solutions (a whole line of them, \(\mathbf x_0+\operatorname{Ker}\)) — never a unique one. This is exactly the bridge into the next block.
18:10–19:00 The Rank-Nullity Theorem ~50 MIN · EZ Ch.5.6 · prove from scratch
Problem 3  ·  12 points

A signal-processing pipeline applies a linear map \(L:V\to W\) between finite-dimensional spaces; engineers care about how much information it keeps (the range) versus destroys (the kernel). The accountant's identity that balances the two is the Rank-Nullity Theorem. Reconstruct it cold.

  1. State the theorem precisely, and define \(\operatorname{rank}(L)\), nullity, \(\operatorname{Ker}(L)\) and \(\operatorname{Ran}(L)\). [2 pts]
  2. Prove it from scratch (no appeal to "rank normal form" as a black box). [5 pts]
  3. A matrix \(A\) is \(3\times5\) with \(\operatorname{rank}(A)=2\). Find the nullity, decide whether \(A\) (as a map \(\mathbb R^5\to\mathbb R^3\)) is injective and/or surjective, and describe the full solution set of \(A\mathbf x=\mathbf b\) when it is consistent. [3 pts]
  4. Using rank-nullity, prove: if \(\dim V=\dim W<\infty\) then a linear map \(L:V\to W\) is injective \(\iff\) it is surjective. [2 pts]
Reveal answer
(a) \(\operatorname{Ker}(L)=\{v\in V: Lv=0\}\) and \(\operatorname{Ran}(L)=\{Lv: v\in V\}\subseteq W\); both are subspaces. Set \(\operatorname{rank}(L)=\dim\operatorname{Ran}(L)\) and \(\text{nullity}(L)=\dim\operatorname{Ker}(L)\). Theorem: \[\dim V=\operatorname{rank}(L)+\text{nullity}(L)=\dim\operatorname{Ran}(L)+\dim\operatorname{Ker}(L).\] (b) Proof (basis-extension). Let \(\dim V=n\) and \(\dim\operatorname{Ker}(L)=k\). Pick a basis \(\{u_1,\dots,u_k\}\) of \(\operatorname{Ker}(L)\) and extend it to a basis \(\{u_1,\dots,u_k,w_1,\dots,w_{n-k}\}\) of \(V\). Claim: \(\{Lw_1,\dots,Lw_{n-k}\}\) is a basis of \(\operatorname{Ran}(L)\).
Spanning: any \(Lv\) with \(v=\sum a_iu_i+\sum b_jw_j\) gives \(Lv=\sum b_j\,Lw_j\) (the \(u_i\) die), so the \(Lw_j\) span \(\operatorname{Ran}(L)\).
Independence: if \(\sum c_j Lw_j=0\) then \(L\!\left(\sum c_jw_j\right)=0\), so \(\sum c_jw_j\in\operatorname{Ker}(L)=\operatorname{span}\{u_i\}\); writing it as \(\sum d_iu_i\) and using independence of the full basis forces all \(c_j=0\) (and \(d_i=0\)). Hence \(\dim\operatorname{Ran}(L)=n-k\), i.e. \(\operatorname{rank}+\text{nullity}=n=\dim V\). \(\blacksquare\) (c) Here \(\dim V=5\), so nullity \(=5-2=3\). Since \(\operatorname{rank}=2<3=\dim(\text{codomain})\), \(A\) is not surjective; since nullity \(=3\neq0\), \(A\) is not injective. When \(A\mathbf x=\mathbf b\) is consistent with one solution \(\mathbf x_0\), the full solution set is the affine translate \(\mathbf x_0+\operatorname{Ker}(A)\) — a 3-dimensional family (three free parameters). (d) \(L\) injective \(\iff\operatorname{Ker}(L)=\{0\}\iff\text{nullity}=0\). By rank-nullity, \(\operatorname{rank}(L)=\dim V-\text{nullity}\), so nullity \(=0\) \(\iff\operatorname{rank}(L)=\dim V=\dim W\) \(\iff\dim\operatorname{Ran}(L)=\dim W\iff\operatorname{Ran}(L)=W\iff L\) surjective. Thus injective \(\iff\) surjective. \(\blacksquare\)

After you finish: log any slip in your Error Log (Conceptual / Formula / Algebra / Time / Misread). The most common trap here is mixing up the direction of \(I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B}\) — its columns are the old basis in new coordinates.
