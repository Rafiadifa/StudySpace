Polynomials of a Matrix — Hard
Lin Alg Final25 June 2026
Cayley–Hamilton & polynomials of a matrix. Every matrix satisfies its own characteristic polynomial: \(p(A)=\mathbf 0\). Consequences: (1) \(A^{-1}\) is a polynomial in \(A\) (solve \(p(A)=0\) for \(I\)); (2) any power \(A^{k}\ (k\ge n)\) reduces to a combination of \(I,A,\dots,A^{n-1}\); (3) \(\{p(A):p\text{ polynomial}\}=\operatorname{span}\{I,A,\dots,A^{m-1}\}\) where \(m=\deg\) of the minimal polynomial.
★ Connected ProblemThe Cayley–Hamilton toolkitpast-final · hard
Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\0&3\end{pmatrix}\).
  1. (a)
    Find the characteristic polynomial.
    Solution\(\det(A-\lambda I)=(2-\lambda)(3-\lambda)=\lambda^2-5\lambda+6\).
    \(\lambda^2-5\lambda+6\).
  2. (b)
    Verify Cayley–Hamilton: \(A^2-5A+6I=0\).
    Solution\(A^2=\begin{pmatrix}4&5\\0&9\end{pmatrix}\); \(5A=\begin{pmatrix}10&5\\0&15\end{pmatrix}\); \(A^2-5A+6I=\begin{pmatrix}4-10+6&5-5\\0&9-15+6\end{pmatrix}=\mathbf 0\). ✓
    \(=\mathbf 0\).
  3. (c)
    Express \(A^{-1}\) as a polynomial in \(A\).
    SolutionFrom \(A^2-5A+6I=0\): \(6I=5A-A^2\Rightarrow I=A\cdot\tfrac{5I-A}{6}\), so \(A^{-1}=\tfrac{1}{6}(5I-A)\). (Check: \(=\begin{pmatrix}1/2&-1/6\\0&1/3\end{pmatrix}\).)
    \(A^{-1}=\tfrac16(5I-A)\).
  4. (d)
    Reduce \(A^3\) to the form \(aA+bI\).
    Solution\(A^2=5A-6I\Rightarrow A^3=A\cdot A^2=5A^2-6A=5(5A-6I)-6A=19A-30I\). (Check: \(\begin{pmatrix}8&19\\0&27\end{pmatrix}\).)
    \(A^3=19A-30I\).
Problem 1Matrix polynomial = 0 (P2-style)hard
A \(4\times4\) matrix \(C\) has characteristic polynomial \(p(\lambda)=(\lambda-2)(\lambda-1)(\lambda^2-2\lambda+x^2)\). Let \(M=(2I-C)(I-C)\) and \(N=C^2-2C+x^2 I\). Compute \(MN\).
Solution\(M=(2I-C)(I-C)=(C-2I)(C-I)\) (two sign flips cancel), and \(N=C^2-2C+x^2I\) is exactly the quadratic factor. So \(MN=(C-2I)(C-I)(C^2-2C+x^2I)=p(C)\). By Cayley–Hamilton \(p(C)=\mathbf 0\).
\(MN=\mathbf 0\).
Problem 2Basis for \(\{p(A)\}\) (P3-style)hard
For \(A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\0&3\end{pmatrix}\), find a basis for \(V=\{p(A):p\text{ is a polynomial}\}\) and \(\dim V\). Prove spanning and independence.
Solution\(A\) has distinct eigenvalues \(2,3\), so its minimal polynomial \(=(\lambda-2)(\lambda-3)\) has degree \(2\). Spanning: by Cayley–Hamilton \(A^2=5A-6I\), so every power (hence every \(p(A)\)) lies in \(\operatorname{span}\{I,A\}\). Independence: \(A\) is not a scalar multiple of \(I\) (off-diagonal \(1\neq0\)), so \(\{I,A\}\) is independent. Thus \(\{I,A\}\) is a basis, \(\dim V=2\).
\(\{I,A\}\), \(\dim=2\).
Problem 3Inverse as a polynomial (P3-style)medium
For \(A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\0&3\end{pmatrix}\), find a degree-1 polynomial \(p\) with \((A+I)^{-1}=p(A)\).
Solution\(M=A+I=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\0&4\end{pmatrix}\) has char poly \((\lambda-3)(\lambda-4)=\lambda^2-7\lambda+12\). Cayley–Hamilton: \(M^2-7M+12I=0\Rightarrow M^{-1}=\tfrac{1}{12}(7I-M)=\tfrac{1}{12}(7I-(A+I))=\tfrac{1}{12}(6I-A)\). So \(p(A)=\tfrac{1}{12}(6I-A)\). (Check: \(\begin{pmatrix}1/3&-1/12\\0&1/4\end{pmatrix}\).)
\((A+I)^{-1}=\tfrac1{12}(6I-A)\).
