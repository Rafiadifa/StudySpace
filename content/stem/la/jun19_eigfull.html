Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}\).
chains: characteristic polynomial · eigenvectors · diagonalisation · matrix power
  1. (a)
    Find the characteristic polynomial and eigenvalues.
    Solution\(\lambda^2-6\lambda+8=0\Rightarrow\lambda=4,2\).
    \(\lambda=4,2\).
  2. (b)
    Find eigenvectors and write \(A=PDP^{-1}\).
    Solution\(\lambda=4:(1,1);\ \lambda=2:(1,-1)\). \(P=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix},D=\operatorname{diag}(4,2)\).
    \(A=PDP^{-1}\).
  3. (c)
    Use it to compute \(A^n\) in closed form.
    Solution\(A^n=PD^nP^{-1}=\tfrac12\begin{pmatrix}4^n+2^n&4^n-2^n\\4^n-2^n&4^n+2^n\end{pmatrix}\).
    as shown.
  4. (d)
    Is \(A\) positive definite? Why?
    SolutionSymmetric with eigenvalues \(4,2>0\Rightarrow\) yes.
    Positive definite.
Problem 1Compute & diagonalise15 pts
\(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&1\end{pmatrix}\). Find \(P,D\) with \(A=PDP^{-1}\).
Solution\(\lambda^2-2\lambda-3=0\Rightarrow\lambda=3,-1\). \(\lambda=3\): \(v=(1,1)\); \(\lambda=-1\): \(v=(1,-1)\). So \(P=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\), \(D=\begin{pmatrix}3&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\).
\(A=PDP^{-1}\) with those \(P,D\).
Symmetric ⇒ \(P\) can be chosen orthogonal after normalising columns.
Problem 2Powers via diagonalisation10 pts
Using Problem 1, find \(A^n\) symbolically.
Solution\(A^n=PD^nP^{-1}\) with \(D^n=\begin{pmatrix}3^n&0\\0&(-1)^n\end{pmatrix}\). \(P^{-1}=\tfrac12\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\).
\(A^n=\tfrac12\begin{pmatrix}3^n+(-1)^n&3^n-(-1)^n\\3^n-(-1)^n&3^n+(-1)^n\end{pmatrix}\).
★ ChallengeExam-level · complex eigenvalues = rotation + scalingpast-final level
Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&-2\\2&1\end{pmatrix}\).
chains: characteristic polynomial · complex eigenpair · geometric meaning · trace/det check
  1. (a)
    Find the eigenvalues.
    Solution\(\det(A-\lambda I)=(1-\lambda)^2+4=\lambda^2-2\lambda+5=0\Rightarrow\lambda=1\pm2i\).
    \(\lambda=1\pm2i\).
  2. (b)
    Find a (complex) eigenvector for \(\lambda=1+2i\).
    Solution\((A-(1+2i)I)=\begin{pmatrix}-2i&-2\\2&-2i\end{pmatrix}\); row 1 gives \(-2i\,v_1-2v_2=0\Rightarrow v_2=-i\,v_1\).
    \(\mathbf v=(1,\,-i)\).
  3. (c)
    Geometrically, what does \(A\) do?
    Solution\(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&-2\\2&1\end{pmatrix}=r\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&-\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}\) with \(r=|\lambda|=\sqrt5\), \(\cos\theta=\tfrac1{\sqrt5},\sin\theta=\tfrac2{\sqrt5}\): a rotation by \(\theta=\arctan2\) and scaling by \(\sqrt5\).
    rotate by \(\arctan2\), scale by \(\sqrt5\).
  4. (d)
    Verify with trace and determinant.
    Solution\(\lambda+\bar\lambda=2=\operatorname{tr}A\) ✓; \(\lambda\bar\lambda=|\lambda|^2=5=\det A\) ✓. (No real eigenvectors ⇒ \(A\) is not diagonalisable over \(\mathbb R\).)
    \(\operatorname{tr}=2,\ \det=5\).
