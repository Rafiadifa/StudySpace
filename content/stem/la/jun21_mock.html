Problem 1Basics6 pts
Warm-ups connecting eigenvalues, determinants, and dependence.
chains: eigenvalues ↔ det/trace · determinant rules · linear dependence
  1. (a)
    A \(3\times3\) real \(A\) has eigenvalues \(-1,1,2\). Find \(\det A\) and \(\operatorname{tr}(A^2)\). 2 pts
    Solution \(\det A=(-1)(1)(2)=-2\). \(A^2\) has eigenvalues \(1,1,4\), so \(\operatorname{tr}(A^2)=6\).
    \(\det A=-2,\ \operatorname{tr}(A^2)=6\).
  2. (b)
    \(B\) is \(3\times3\) with \(\det B=4\). Find \(\det(2B^{-1})\). 2 pts
    Solution \(\det(2B^{-1})=2^3\det(B^{-1})=8\cdot\tfrac14=2\).
    \(2\).
  3. (c)
    Find \(k\) making \(u=(1,0,1),v=(1,1,0),w=(0,k,1)\) linearly dependent. 2 pts
    Solution \(\det\begin{pmatrix}1&1&0\\0&1&k\\1&0&1\end{pmatrix}=1(1-0)-1(0-k)+0=1+k\). Dependent iff \(=0\Rightarrow k=-1\).
    \(k=-1\).
Problem 2The variable-parameter matrix10 pts
Let \(C_x=\begin{pmatrix}1&1&0&1\\0&2&0&0\\0&1&1&x\\1&1&-x&1\end{pmatrix}\) depend on a real \(x\).
chains: determinant structure · characteristic polynomial · complex eigenvector · Cayley–Hamilton
  1. (a)
    Without full expansion, find the coefficient of \(x^2\) in \(\det(C_x)\). 2 pts
    Solution The only \(x\)'s sit in positions \((3,4)=x\) and \((4,3)=-x\). A term reaching \(x^2\) must use both; the complementary \(2\times2\) minor from rows \(1,2\)/cols \(1,2\) is \(\det\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&2\end{pmatrix}=2\), and the two entries contribute \((x)(-x)\) with the appropriate sign giving \(+2x^2\).
    Coefficient of \(x^2\) is \(2\).
  2. (b)
    Show the characteristic polynomial is \(p(\lambda)=(\lambda-2)(\lambda-1)(\lambda^2-2\lambda+x^2)\). For which \(x\) is \(C_x\) singular? 3 pts
    Solution Cofactor-expand \(\det(\lambda I-C_x)\); the second row/column structure peels off factors \((\lambda-2)\) and \((\lambda-1)\), leaving the \(2\times2\) block on the \((1,4)\) coordinates giving \(\lambda^2-2\lambda+x^2\). Singular ⇔ \(0\) is an eigenvalue ⇔ \(p(0)=(-2)(-1)(x^2)=2x^2=0\Rightarrow x=0\).
    \(C_x\) singular iff \(x=0\).
  3. (c)
    For \(x=2\), find all four eigenvalues and a complex eigenvector for the one with positive imaginary part. 3 pts
    Solution Eigenvalues \(2,1\) and roots of \(\lambda^2-2\lambda+4=0\Rightarrow\lambda=1\pm i\sqrt3\). For \(\lambda=1+i\sqrt3\), solve \((C_2-\lambda I)\mathbf v=0\); a valid eigenvector is \(\mathbf v=(1,\,0,\,i\sqrt3,\,1)^{\!T}\) type — explicitly any nonzero solution of the reduced system (e.g. \(\mathbf v=( -1,\,0,\,1,\,\tfrac{1-i\sqrt3}{2})^T\) after row-reduction).
    \(\lambda\in\{2,1,1\pm i\sqrt3\}\).
    Full credit: any nonzero \(\mathbf v\) with \((C_2-(1+i\sqrt3)I)\mathbf v=0\), shown by back-substitution.
  4. (d)
    Let \(M=(2I-C_x)(I-C_x)\), \(N=C_x^2-2C_x+x^2I\). Compute \(MN\) without multiplying entrywise. 2 pts
    Solution \(M=(2I-C_x)(I-C_x)=(C_x-2I)(C_x-I)\) and \(N=C_x^2-2C_x+x^2I\). So \(MN=(C_x-2I)(C_x-I)(C_x^2-2C_x+x^2I)=p(C_x)\). By Cayley–Hamilton \(p(C_x)=0\).
    \(MN=\mathbf 0\).
Problem 3Symmetric structure & polynomials of A11 pts
Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}2&0&1\\0&c&0\\1&0&2\end{pmatrix}\) with real parameter \(c\).
chains: spectral decomposition · definiteness · the algebra \(\{p(A)\}\) · inverse as a polynomial
  1. (a)
    For generic \(c\), find \(Q\) orthogonal and \(\Lambda\) diagonal with \(A=Q\Lambda Q^T\). 4 pts
    Solution The \((1,3)\) block \(\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix}\) gives \(3\) with \((1,0,1)\) and \(1\) with \((1,0,-1)\); the middle gives \(c\) with \((0,1,0)\). Normalise: \[Q=\begin{pmatrix}\tfrac1{\sqrt2}&0&\tfrac1{\sqrt2}\\0&1&0\\\tfrac1{\sqrt2}&0&-\tfrac1{\sqrt2}\end{pmatrix},\ \Lambda=\operatorname{diag}(3,c,1).\]
    \(A=Q\Lambda Q^T\) as above.
  2. (b)
    For which \(c\) is \(A\) indefinite? 2 pts
    Solution Eigenvalues \(3,1,c\). Two are positive, so \(A\) is indefinite iff the third is negative: \(c<0\).
    Indefinite iff \(c<0\).
  3. (c)
    Let \(c=3\). Find a basis for \(V=\{p(A):p\text{ a polynomial}\}\) and prove it. 3 pts
    Solution With \(c=3\), \(A\) has just two distinct eigenvalues \(\{3,1\}\), so its minimal polynomial \((\lambda-3)(\lambda-1)\) has degree \(2\). Every \(p(A)\) reduces mod the minimal polynomial to \(\alpha I+\beta A\). Spanning: by that reduction. Independence: \(\alpha I+\beta A=0\) forces \(\alpha+3\beta=0\) and \(\alpha+\beta=0\) (eigenvalue equations) \(\Rightarrow\alpha=\beta=0\).
    Basis \(\{I,A\}\), \(\dim V=2\).
  4. (d)
    For \(c=3\), find a degree-1 \(p\) with \((A+I)^{-1}=p(A)\). 2 pts
    Solution Want \(p(\lambda)=\alpha\lambda+\beta\) with \(p(\lambda)=\tfrac{1}{\lambda+1}\) at the eigenvalues \(3,1\): \(p(3)=\tfrac14,\ p(1)=\tfrac12\). Solve \(3\alpha+\beta=\tfrac14,\ \alpha+\beta=\tfrac12\Rightarrow\alpha=-\tfrac18,\ \beta=\tfrac58\). So \((A+I)^{-1}=\tfrac{5I-A}{8}\).
    \(p(x)=\tfrac{5-x}{8}\), i.e. \((A+I)^{-1}=\tfrac18(5I-A)\).
Problem 4Signal recovery: SVD, QR, least squares14 pts
Recover \(\mathbf x\in\mathbb R^2\) from \(\mathbf b\in\mathbb R^3\) via \(A\mathbf x=\mathbf b\), \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&0\\1&-1\end{pmatrix},\ \mathbf b=\begin{pmatrix}3\\2\\t\end{pmatrix}.\) The columns of \(A\) happen to be orthogonal.
chains: singular values · range/QR · projection · least squares · SVD ellipse geometry
  1. (a)
    Find the singular values of \(A\). 3 pts
    Solution \(A^TA=\begin{pmatrix}3&0\\0&2\end{pmatrix}\) (columns orthogonal, norms \(\sqrt3,\sqrt2\)). Eigenvalues \(3,2\Rightarrow\sigma_1=\sqrt3,\ \sigma_2=\sqrt2\).
    \(\sigma=\sqrt3,\ \sqrt2\).
  2. (b)
    When is \(\mathbf b\in\operatorname{Ran}(A)\)? Give a QR decomposition of \(A\). 4 pts
    Solution \(\operatorname{Ran}(A)\) is the plane spanned by the columns; its normal is \(a_1\times a_2=(1,1,1)\times(1,0,-1)=(-1,2,-1)\). \(\mathbf b\in\operatorname{Ran}(A)\) iff \((-1,2,-1)\cdot(3,2,t)=1-t=0\), i.e. \(t=1\). Since the columns are already orthogonal, \(A=QR\) with \[Q=\Big[\tfrac{1}{\sqrt3}(1,1,1)^T,\ \tfrac1{\sqrt2}(1,0,-1)^T\Big],\quad R=\begin{pmatrix}\sqrt3&0\\0&\sqrt2\end{pmatrix}.\]
    \(\mathbf b\in\operatorname{Ran}(A)\) iff \(t=1\); \(R=\operatorname{diag}(\sqrt3,\sqrt2)\).
  3. (c)
    Find the orthogonal projection matrix \(P\) onto \(\operatorname{Ran}(A)\). 2 pts
    Solution \(P=QQ^T=\tfrac13(1,1,1)^T(1,1,1)+\tfrac12(1,0,-1)^T(1,0,-1)=\begin{pmatrix}5/6&1/3&-1/6\\1/3&1/3&1/3\\-1/6&1/3&5/6\end{pmatrix}\).
    \(P=QQ^T\) (above). \(I-P\) projects onto the noise direction \((-1,2,-1)\).
  4. (d)
    Find the least-squares solution \(\hat{\mathbf x}\) minimising \(\lVert A\mathbf x-\mathbf b\rVert^2\). 2 pts
    Solution Normal equations \(A^TA\hat{\mathbf x}=A^T\mathbf b\): \(A^T\mathbf b=(3+2+t,\ 3-t)=(5+t,\ 3-t)\). With \(A^TA=\operatorname{diag}(3,2)\): \(\hat{\mathbf x}=\big(\tfrac{5+t}{3},\ \tfrac{3-t}{2}\big)\).
    \(\hat{\mathbf x}=\big(\tfrac{5+t}{3},\tfrac{3-t}{2}\big)\).
  5. (e)
    (Hard) \(B=\begin{pmatrix}2&2\\1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}\). The image \(B(S)\) of the unit circle \(S\subset\mathbb R^2\) is an ellipse in \(\mathbb R^3\). Find the half-axis lengths and their direction vectors. 3 pts
    Solution Axis lengths = singular values of \(B\). \(B^TB=\begin{pmatrix}5&4\\4&5\end{pmatrix}\), eigenvalues \(9,1\Rightarrow\sigma=3,1\). Right singular vectors \(v_1=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(1,1),\ v_2=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(1,-1)\); directions in \(\mathbb R^3\) are \(u_i=\tfrac1{\sigma_i}Bv_i\): \(u_1=\tfrac{1}{3\sqrt2}(4,1,1)\), \(u_2=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(0,1,-1)\).
    Long axis \(3\) along \((4,1,1)\); short axis \(1\) along \((0,1,-1)\).
Problem 5A block matrix from polynomials of A12 pts
Let \(A\) be \(2\times2\) and \(K=\begin{pmatrix}A&A+I\\A+I&A+2I\end{pmatrix}\) (a \(4\times4\) block matrix).
chains: block elimination (det) · block inverse · eigenvector structure · eigenvalues · definiteness
  1. (a)
    Compute \(\det K\) by block Gaussian elimination. 2 pts
    Solution Block \(R_2\to R_2-R_1\): \(\begin{pmatrix}A&A+I\\I&I\end{pmatrix}\). Block \(C_1\to C_1-C_2\): \(\begin{pmatrix}-I&A+I\\0&I\end{pmatrix}\), block upper-triangular. \(\det K=\det(-I)\det(I)=(-1)^2\cdot1=1\).
    \(\det K=1\) (for every \(A\)).
  2. (b)
    Find \(K^{-1}\) in block form in terms of \(A,I\). 3 pts
    Solution Solve \(K\binom{x}{y}=\binom{e}{f}\). Row\(_2-\)Row\(_1\): \(x+y=f-e\). Row\(_1\): \(A(x+y)+y=e\Rightarrow y=e-A(f-e)=(I+A)e-Af\), then \(x=(f-e)-y=-(2I+A)e+(I+A)f\). Hence \[K^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}-(A+2I)&A+I\\A+I&-A\end{pmatrix}.\]
    \(K^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}-(A+2I)&A+I\\A+I&-A\end{pmatrix}\).
  3. (c)
    Let \(A\mathbf v=\lambda\mathbf v\) (\(\lambda\neq-1\)). Find all \(\alpha\) so that \(\mathbf w=\binom{\mathbf v}{\alpha\mathbf v}\) is an eigenvector of \(K\). 3 pts
    Solution \(K\mathbf w=\binom{(\lambda+\alpha(\lambda+1))\mathbf v}{((\lambda+1)+\alpha(\lambda+2))\mathbf v}\). Eigenvector ⇒ second block \(=\alpha\cdot\)first: \((\lambda+1)+\alpha(\lambda+2)=\alpha(\lambda+\alpha(\lambda+1))\), giving \((\lambda+1)(1-\alpha^2)+2\alpha=0\), i.e. \((\lambda+1)\alpha^2-2\alpha-(\lambda+1)=0\). \[\alpha=\frac{1\pm\sqrt{1+(\lambda+1)^2}}{\lambda+1}.\]
    Two values \(\alpha=\dfrac{1\pm\sqrt{1+(\lambda+1)^2}}{\lambda+1}\); note \(\alpha_1\alpha_2=-1\).
  4. (d)
    Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}\). Find all four eigenvalues of \(K\). 2 pts
    Solution \(A\) has eigenvalues \(\lambda=1,0\). Each gives \(\mu=\lambda+\alpha(\lambda+1)\). For \(\lambda=1\): \(\alpha=\tfrac{1\pm\sqrt5}{2}\Rightarrow\mu=1+2\alpha=2\pm\sqrt5\). For \(\lambda=0\): \(\alpha=1\pm\sqrt2\Rightarrow\mu=\alpha=1\pm\sqrt2\).
    \(\{2+\sqrt5,\,2-\sqrt5,\,1+\sqrt2,\,1-\sqrt2\}\) (product \(=1=\det K\) ✓).
  5. (e)
    For which \(A\) is \(K\) positive definite? 2 pts
    Solution From (c), for each eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of (symmetric) \(A\) the two eigenvalues of \(K\) satisfy \(\mu_1\mu_2=-1\) — one positive, one negative. So \(K\) always has a negative eigenvalue.
    \(K\) is positive definite for no \(A\) — the \(\mu_1\mu_2=-1\) pairing forbids it.
Problem 6A linear map on trace-zero matrices9 pts
\(V=\{2\times2\) real \(X:\operatorname{tr}X=0\}\), \(L(X)=JX-XJ\) with \(J=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}\). Basis \(\mathcal B=(E,H,F)\): \(E=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix},H=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix},F=\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\).
chains: matrix of a linear map · eigenvalues/multiplicity/diagonalizability · change of basis
  1. (a)
    Find \([L]_{\mathcal B}\). 3 pts
    Solution \(L(E)=JE-EJ=0\). \(L(H)=JH-HJ=\begin{pmatrix}0&-2\\0&0\end{pmatrix}=-2E\). \(L(F)=JF-FJ=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}=H\). Columns in \(\mathcal B\): \[[L]_{\mathcal B}=\begin{pmatrix}0&-2&0\\0&0&1\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix}.\]
    Strictly upper-triangular as above.
  2. (b)
    Eigenvalues with algebraic & geometric multiplicities. Is \(L\) diagonalizable? 3 pts
    Solution Strictly upper-triangular ⇒ only eigenvalue \(0\), algebraic multiplicity \(3\). Geometric multiplicity \(=3-\operatorname{rank}=3-2=1\). Since \(1<3\), not diagonalizable (\(L\) is nilpotent — a single Jordan block).
    \(\lambda=0\): alg \(3\), geom \(1\); not diagonalizable.
  3. (c)
    \(\mathcal C=(E',H',F')\), \(E'=E,H'=E+H,F'=E+H+F\). Find \(P_{\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal C}\) and \([L]_{\mathcal C}\). 3 pts
    Solution Columns are \(\mathcal C\)-vectors in \(\mathcal B\): \(P_{\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal C}=\begin{pmatrix}1&1&1\\0&1&1\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}\), \(P^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}1&-1&0\\0&1&-1\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}\). Then \([L]_{\mathcal C}=P^{-1}[L]_{\mathcal B}P=\begin{pmatrix}0&-2&-3\\0&0&1\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix}\).
    \([L]_{\mathcal C}=\begin{pmatrix}0&-2&-3\\0&0&1\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix}\) (still nilpotent — similarity preserves it).
